Cell Cycle Checkpoints

This simulation models the eukaryotic cell cycle control system, showing how cyclins, CDKs, and checkpoint proteins coordinate cell division and protect against errors and DNA damage.

Cell Cycle Phases

  • G1 (Gap 1): Cell growth, preparation for DNA synthesis
  • S (Synthesis): DNA replication
  • G2 (Gap 2): Growth and preparation for mitosis
  • M (Mitosis): Cell division

Cyclin-CDK Complexes

  • Cyclin D-CDK4/6: G1 phase, Rb phosphorylation
  • Cyclin E-CDK2: G1/S transition, full Rb inactivation
  • Cyclin A-CDK2: S phase, DNA replication
  • Cyclin B-CDK1: G2/M transition, mitotic entry

Major Checkpoints

  • G1 Checkpoint (Restriction Point): Checks growth factors, cell size, DNA integrity
  • S Checkpoint: Monitors DNA replication fidelity
  • G2 Checkpoint: Verifies complete, undamaged DNA replication
  • M Checkpoint (Spindle Assembly): Ensures proper chromosome attachment

Tumor Suppressors

  • Rb (Retinoblastoma): Blocks E2F transcription factors; inactivated by phosphorylation
  • p53 (Guardian of the Genome): Activated by DNA damage; induces p21 or apoptosis
  • p21: CDK inhibitor; blocks cell cycle progression

DNA Damage Response

  • ATM/ATR: Damage sensors that activate checkpoint kinases
  • CHK1/CHK2: Checkpoint kinases that stabilize p53
  • Cell Fate: Repair ‚Üí continue, or Apoptosis if irreparable

About This Simulation

Key Concepts

  • Enzyme Kinetics: Enzymes catalyze reactions following Michaelis-Menten kinetics, with rates determined by substrate concentration and enzyme affinity (Km).
  • Lock and Key vs Induced Fit: Enzymes recognize substrates either through rigid complementarity (lock-key) or conformational changes upon binding (induced fit).
  • Allosteric Regulation: Enzyme activity modulated by molecules binding at sites other than the active site, enabling sophisticated metabolic control.
  • Signal Transduction: Cascades of molecular interactions that amplify and transmit signals from cell surface to nucleus.

Why It Matters

Molecular understanding enables drug design, metabolic engineering, and synthetic biology.
