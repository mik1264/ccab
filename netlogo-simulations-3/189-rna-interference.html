Overview

RNA interference is a conserved biological mechanism where small RNA molecules silence gene expression by targeting complementary mRNA for degradation or translational repression.

Types of Small RNAs

  • siRNA (small interfering RNA): ~21-23 nt, perfect complementarity, primarily induces mRNA cleavage
  • miRNA (microRNA): ~22 nt, imperfect base pairing, primarily blocks translation
  • piRNA: Longer, protects germline from transposons

The RNAi Pathway

  1. Dicer processing: Double-stranded RNA is cleaved into small duplexes
  2. RISC loading: One strand (guide) is loaded into Argonaute protein
  3. Target recognition: Guide RNA base-pairs with complementary mRNA
  4. Silencing: mRNA is cleaved (siRNA) or translation is blocked (miRNA)

RISC Components

  • Argonaute (AGO): Core protein, AGO2 has "slicer" endonuclease activity
  • Guide strand: The small RNA that provides target specificity
  • Accessory proteins: GW182, P-bodies for mRNA degradation

siRNA vs miRNA

siRNA: Perfect match ‚Üí AGO2 cleaves mRNA ‚Üí rapid degradation

miRNA: Seed region (nt 2-8) match ‚Üí deadenylation ‚Üí gradual decay + translation block

Applications

  • Research: Gene knockdown studies
  • Therapeutics: FDA-approved siRNA drugs (e.g., Patisiran)
  • Agriculture: Pest-resistant crops
