Lock and Key Model (Emil Fischer, 1894)

The lock-and-key model proposes that the enzyme's active site has a rigid, fixed shape that perfectly complements the substrate - like a key fitting into a lock. Only substrates with the exact complementary shape can bind.

  • Rigid enzyme: No conformational change occurs
  • Perfect fit: Substrate shape exactly matches active site
  • Selectivity: Explains enzyme specificity
  • Limitation: Cannot explain transition state stabilization

Induced Fit Model (Daniel Koshland, 1958)

The induced-fit model suggests that both enzyme and substrate undergo conformational changes upon binding. The active site molds around the substrate, creating an even tighter fit and stabilizing the transition state.

  • Flexible enzyme: Active site changes shape during binding
  • Mutual adaptation: Both enzyme and substrate adjust
  • Transition state: Better explains catalytic mechanism
  • Energy: Reduces activation energy more effectively

Michaelis-Menten Kinetics

Both models follow Michaelis-Menten kinetics:

  • Km (Michaelis constant): Substrate concentration at half-maximal velocity
  • Vmax: Maximum reaction rate when all enzymes are saturated
  • Lower Km = higher enzyme affinity for substrate
