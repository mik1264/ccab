Wealth Distribution

Structural Inequality from Heterogeneous Foraging

About This Gallery

The Wealth Distribution model extends Sugarscape concepts to focus specifically on emergent economic inequality from initially equal opportunities. Unlike pure Sugarscape, this variant maintains constant population by replacing dead agents immediately, allowing long-term statistical analysis of wealth distributions without population collapse.

Core Mechanics: Agents possess vision (how far they can see to find food) and metabolism (how much food they consume per tick). Both vary across individuals but remain fixed for each agent's lifetime. The environment distributes grain patches with varying maximum capacity (0-50 units), growing at +1 unit per tick until reaching capacity.

Key Insight: Fitness advantages compound dramatically over time. High-vision agents spot richer patches from farther away. Low-metabolism agents burn through wealth slowly. Agents with high vision and low metabolism combine both advantages, dominating the best patches and accumulating substantial wealth reserves. Meanwhile, low-vision high-metabolism agents struggle to survive.

Watch inequality emerge through Lorenz curves (cumulative wealth distribution), Gini coefficients (0.4-0.6 typical), and spatial segregation as wealthy agents monopolize prime territory. This demonstrates how structural factors generate persistent inequality even in theoretically fair systems.

Educational Value

This gallery demonstrates that structural factors generate persistent inequality even in theoretically fair systems. All agents follow identical behavioral rules. Initial wealth distributes equally (or randomly within small ranges). Yet outcomes diverge dramatically based on intrinsic attributes (vision, metabolism) that could represent education, health, or luck.

The system lacks mechanisms for wealth redistribution or social mobility in the base model, allowing initial advantages to compound indefinitely. Policy experiments with taxation, inheritance, and social programs test whether interventions reduce inequality or whether competitive dynamics overwhelm redistributive efforts.

Technologies: Pure HTML5 Canvas rendering with vanilla JavaScript. No external libraries. Real-time computation of Gini coefficients, Lorenz curves, and spatial statistics.
