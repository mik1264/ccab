Renaissance Calculators

The first mechanical minds — 1597 to 1694

Before electricity, before transistors, before even the concept of a “computer,” brilliant minds built machines of brass, ivory, and wood that could add, multiply, and divide. From Napier’s numbered rods to Leibniz’s stepped drum, these Renaissance calculators laid the foundations of mechanical computation. Explore six landmark devices that bridged the gap between abacus and algorithm.

Mechanical Pioneers

Six interactive recreations of history's first calculating machines, spanning a century of ingenuity from 1597 to 1694.

Interactive
1

Napier's Bones (1617)

Drag and arrange numbered ivory rods to perform lattice multiplication. Watch diagonal sums reveal products instantly — John Napier’s elegant shortcut for merchants and astronomers.

 Interactive
2

Galileo's Sector (1597)

Open and close a proportional compass to solve proportions, multiply, divide, and compute trigonometric values. The geometric principles behind each operation come alive.

 Interactive
3

Schickard's Calculating Clock (1623)

The first known mechanical calculator: turn toothed wheels to add six-digit numbers and watch automatic carry propagation ripple through the mechanism.

 Interactive
4

Pascal's Pascaline (1642)

Spin stylus-driven brass wheels to enter numbers and see Blaise Pascal’s ingenious tens-carry mechanism at work. Peer inside the interlocking gears.

 Interactive
5

Leibniz Stepped Reckoner (1694)

The first calculator for all four operations. See the revolutionary stepped drum mechanism engage with counting wheels to add, subtract, multiply, and divide.

 Interactive
6

Morland's Calculators (1666)

Switch between Samuel Morland’s three calculating instruments: trigonometry, multiplication, and addition. Flat disc designs that became the first English calculators.
