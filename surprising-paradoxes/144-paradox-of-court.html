The Original Contract

The problem? Euathlus finished his education but never took any cases. Years passed. Protagoras grew impatient and decided to sue‚Äîbut the very act of suing created the paradox!

Historical Resolution

"The jurors, thinking that the plea on both sides was uncertain and insoluble, for fear that their decision, for whichever side it was rendered, might annul itself, left the matter undecided and postponed the case to a distant day."
‚Äî Aulus Gellius, Attic Nights (2nd century CE)

The court essentially threw up its hands! They recognized that any ruling would create a contradiction and refused to decide.

Modern Analysis

The Temporal Solution

The contract and court ruling apply at different times. The contract triggers AFTER a win; the court ruling applies NOW. So if Euathlus wins, he doesn't pay now (court ruling) but must pay later (contract triggers).

The Priority Solution

Court rulings supersede private contracts. If the court says "don't pay," that's final. The contract clause is rendered unenforceable.

The Premature Suit

Protagoras sued too early! The contract says "after winning a case"‚Äîthis case doesn't count as it's about the contract itself. Protagoras should wait for Euathlus to win a REAL case, then sue.

The Self-Reference Problem

Like the Liar Paradox, the problem is self-reference. The case is about itself. Some logicians argue such self-referential propositions are simply meaningless.

Why It Still Matters

This 2,500-year-old puzzle remains relevant in modern law:

  • Conditional contracts: What happens when conditions become self-referential?
  • Judicial authority: Can courts override private agreements?
  • Legal bootstrapping: Can a lawsuit create the conditions for its own success?

The paradox appears in modern deontic logic (the logic of obligation) and remains a test case for theories of legal reasoning.
