The Baader-Meinhof Phenomenon

Paradox #453 • Arnold Zwicky, 2005

The Frequency Illusion

Have you ever learned a new word—and then suddenly heard it three times that same week? Or bought a car and started seeing the same model everywhere?

It feels like the universe is sending you messages. But it's actually a cognitive bias called the Frequency Illusion (or Baader-Meinhof Phenomenon).

The things aren't appearing more often. You're just noticing them now.

Let's demonstrate this with an experiment.

The Origin Story

The name "Baader-Meinhof" comes from a 1994 letter to the St. Paul Pioneer Press. The writer, Terry Mullen, mentioned learning about the German Baader-Meinhof gang—and then hearing about them multiple times that week.

Stanford linguist Arnold Zwicky gave it the formal name "Frequency Illusion" in 2005, alongside its cousin the "Recency Illusion" (believing new things are actually new).

Why It Happens

Two cognitive processes combine:

  • Selective Attention: Once something is in your working memory, your brain flags it as important. Your attention naturally gravitates to it.
  • Confirmation Bias: Each new sighting reinforces the feeling that it's "everywhere." You don't notice all the times it WASN'T there.

Why This Matters

The frequency illusion affects how we perceive trends, threats, and opportunities. After one news story about a rare disease, we might feel it's an epidemic. After buying a product, we "see" it everywhere—reinforcing our purchase decision.

Marketers exploit this: once you notice a brand, you'll keep noticing it, making it seem more popular than it is.

Breaking the Illusion

Remember: the world didn't change—your attention did. When something suddenly seems "everywhere," ask yourself: "Was it always this common, and I just started paying attention?"
