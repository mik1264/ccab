Three Key Experiments:

Experiment 1 - Poetry: Participants rated a poem described as taking "18 hours" significantly higher in quality than the identical poem described as taking "4 hours."

Experiment 2 - Paintings: The same painting was valued at $21,456 when described as taking "26 hours" vs. $16,423 when described as taking "4 hours."

Experiment 3 - Armor: Medieval armor described as taking "110 hours" was rated higher quality than armor described as taking "15 hours"‚Äî especially when the image was low-resolution (harder to judge actual quality).

üß† Why Does This Happen?

The effort heuristic likely evolved because effort usually correlates with quality. A hunter who tracked prey for days probably has better meat. A craftsman who spent weeks on a tool probably made something durable. But in the modern world, this heuristic misfires:

  • Efficiency is invisible: An expert who solves a problem in 5 minutes may have spent decades learning how
  • Busywork signals dedication: We confuse "working hard" with "working effectively"
  • Effort justifies cost: We accept higher prices when we believe more labor went in

üîó Related Biases

  • IKEA Effect: We value things we built ourselves more than pre-made items
  • Labor Illusion: We trust services more when they show visible "work"
  • Sunk Cost Fallacy: Past effort makes us reluctant to abandon projects
  • Just-World Hypothesis: We believe hard work deserves reward
