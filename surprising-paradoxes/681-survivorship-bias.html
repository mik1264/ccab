Survivorship Bias

During WWII, the military wanted to armor their planes. They studied returning aircraft and found bullet holes concentrated on the fuselage. So they should armor the fuselage, right? WRONG.

The obvious conclusion: "Armor the fuselage! That's where they're getting hit!"

‚ùå The Wrong Thinking

"We see lots of bullet holes here, so this area must be vulnerable. Let's add armor where we see damage!"

This ignores the planes that DIDN'T return ‚Äî the ones with damage in places we never got to observe.

"We DON'T see bullet holes on the engines. But bullets hit randomly. So where are the engine-hit planes?"

Answer: They crashed. The absence of damage in returning planes reveals what damage is FATAL.

How to Avoid Survivorship Bias

1. Ask "What's missing?" ‚Äî Always consider the entities that didn't survive the selection process. What happened to failures?

2. Seek out failure data ‚Äî Study failed companies, dropped stocks, closed funds. The dead can teach us more than the living.

3. Be skeptical of success stories ‚Äî For every success story, there may be thousands of identical attempts that failed. Success might be luck, not strategy.

4. Consider base rates ‚Äî Before concluding that a strategy works, ask how many people tried it and what fraction succeeded.

Abraham Wald's Legacy

Abraham Wald (1902-1950) was a Hungarian-American mathematician who fled Nazi Europe. At Columbia University's Statistical Research Group during WWII, his aircraft analysis saved countless lives and pioneered operations research.

His insight ‚Äî that missing data is often the most important data ‚Äî remains one of the most valuable lessons in statistics. The military continued using his methods through the Vietnam War.

Tragically, Wald died in a plane crash in 1950, at age 48. The irony was not lost on statisticians.
