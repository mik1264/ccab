üî¨ The Original Experiments

Zajonc (1968) showed participants Chinese characters with varying frequencies (0, 1, 2, 5, 10, 25 times). The more often a character appeared, the more participants "liked" it‚Äîeven though they couldn't distinguish seen from unseen characters!

Kunst-Wilson & Zajonc (1980) flashed shapes for just 1 millisecond‚Äîtoo fast to consciously perceive. Participants still rated the exposed shapes as more "likable" while having ZERO memory of seeing them. Affect came before cognition!

The Paradox: We think we like things because of their objective qualities. But often, we like them simply because they're familiar‚Äîand we confuse "easy to process" with "good."

üí° Why Does This Happen?

Processing Fluency: Familiar stimuli are easier to process. This fluency feels good, and we misattribute that positive feeling to the stimulus itself.

Safety Signal: Evolutionarily, familiar things haven't killed you yet. New = potential danger. Mere exposure may be an ancient survival heuristic: "Known = Safe = Good."

Perceptual Fluency ‚Üí Positive Affect: The brain rewards itself for successful prediction. Familiar patterns are easier to predict, triggering micro-rewards that we experience as liking.
