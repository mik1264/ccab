The Paradox of Rewards

Common sense says: Want someone to do something more? Reward them!

But psychology reveals a dark side: external rewards can destroy internal motivation.

üéØThe Critical Distinction

Not all rewards are equally damaging. The key factors:

Most Damaging

  • Expected rewards ‚Äî known in advance
  • Tangible rewards ‚Äî money, prizes, grades
  • Contingent on doing ‚Äî "If you do X, you get Y"
  • Controlling language ‚Äî "You should," "You must"

Less Damaging

  • Unexpected rewards ‚Äî surprises after the fact
  • Verbal praise ‚Äî recognition without material reward
  • Competence feedback ‚Äî "You're really good at this!"
  • Autonomy support ‚Äî "Would you like to..."
Key insight: It's not rewards themselves that are harmful‚Äîit's rewards that feel controlling or shift the perceived reason for behavior from internal to external.

üìñThe Tom Sawyer Principle

Mark Twain understood this long before the research:

Work consists of whatever a body is obliged to do, and play consists of whatever a body is not obliged to do.

‚Äî Mark Twain, "The Adventures of Tom Sawyer" (1876)

Tom got his friends to pay him for the privilege of whitewashing the fence‚Äîby making it seem like a rare opportunity rather than a chore. He understood that the framing of an activity determines whether it feels like work or play.

The overjustification effect is the reverse: turning play into work by adding external reasons to do it.
