Jerry Harvey's Original Story

Management professor Jerry B. Harvey coined the term in 1974, based on his own family experience. After the miserable trip, his father-in-law admitted he only suggested it because he thought the others were bored. His mother-in-law said she went along to please everyone. His wife confessed she'd been miserable the whole time.

Harvey's epiphany: "Here we were, four reasonably sensible people who, of our own volition, had just taken a 106-mile trip across a godforsaken desert in furnace-like temperatures to eat unpalatable food at a hole-in-the-wall cafeteria‚Äîand none of us had really wanted to go."

How It Differs from Groupthink

In groupthink, people genuinely convince themselves the group is right. In the Abilene Paradox, everyone KNOWS they disagree‚Äîbut assumes they're alone in that disagreement. It's a failure of communication, not a distortion of individual views.

Real-World Consequences

Harvey cited the Watergate cover-up as a potential Abilene Paradox. Multiple participants later said they had personal doubts but assumed everyone else supported the cover-up.

The Challenger disaster showed similar patterns: engineers had concerns about launching in cold weather, but management's apparent eagerness silenced dissent.

Why It Happens

  • Fear of separation: Being the only dissenter feels risky
  • Negative fantasies: We imagine terrible consequences of speaking up
  • Pluralistic ignorance: We misread others' silent compliance as genuine enthusiasm
  • Fear of blame: If the alternative fails, the dissenter gets blamed

The Solution: Psychological Safety

The Abilene Paradox dissolves when one person simply asks: "Does everyone ACTUALLY want to do this? Let's vote privately." Creating space for honest dissent prevents the collective march toward outcomes nobody wants.
