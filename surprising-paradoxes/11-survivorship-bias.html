Survivorship Bias

We only see the winners, never the losers. This invisible selection creates dangerous illusions about what leads to success.

The WWII Aircraft Puzzle

During World War II, the US military asked statistician Abraham Wald to help reinforce aircraft. They showed him where returning bombers had bullet holes and planned to add armor there. Wald's response? "Armor the places with NO bullet holes."

Where Should We Add Armor?

Returning Aircraft (What We See)

Military's Plan: Armor Here

Military's Intuition

"Add armor where we see bullet holes - those areas are getting hit!"

Wald's Insight

"Planes hit in those areas SURVIVED. The missing planes were hit in the areas with NO holes - the cockpit and engines!"

The bullet holes showed where planes could be hit and still survive. The military was only seeing the survivors - the planes that made it back. The planes hit in critical areas (cockpit, engines) never returned to be studied.

This is survivorship bias: drawing conclusions from only the visible successes while ignoring the invisible failures.

Survivorship Bias Everywhere

Success Stories

"Bill Gates dropped out of college!" But so did thousands of others who aren't billionaires. You never hear their stories.

Old Buildings

"They don't build them like they used to!" Actually, they do. The poorly-built old buildings already collapsed - you only see the survivors.

Music & Art

"Music was better in the 60s!" No, the bad 60s music was forgotten. Only the best survived to be remembered.

Investment Funds

Fund performance looks great - but failed funds are closed and their poor returns disappear from the averages.

Entrepreneurs

"Follow your passion!" say successful people. But many who followed their passion failed silently.

Medicine History

"Ancient remedies work!" The ones that didn't work were abandoned. Only effective treatments survived to the present.

How to Fight Survivorship Bias

1. Ask about the failures: "How many people tried this and failed?"

2. Look for the missing data: "Who's NOT in this sample, and why?"

3. Be skeptical of success formulas: What works for survivors might not work in general.

4. Consider base rates: What's the overall success rate, not just among those you can see?

Remember: You can only learn from history that was recorded. You can only study what survived. The silent majority of failures have much to teach us - if only we could see them.
