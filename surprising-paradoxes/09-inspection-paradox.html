The Inspection Paradox

Why does the bus always seem late? If buses come every 10 minutes on average, why do you often wait longer than 5 minutes? It's not bad luck - it's mathematics!

The Bus Waiting Paradox

If buses arrive every 10 minutes on average, you might expect to wait 5 minutes on average when you arrive at a random time. But the actual average wait is often longer than 5 minutes! This counterintuitive result happens because you're more likely to arrive during a long gap than a short one.

The Key Insight: Size-Biased Sampling

When you arrive at a random time, you're sampling intervals proportionally to their length. A 15-minute gap takes up three times as much "time" as a 5-minute gap, so you're three times as likely to land in it!

This is called size-biased sampling or the length-biased sampling problem. You're not sampling bus intervals uniformly - you're sampling them weighted by their duration.

The Paradox Everywhere

Class Size

Ask students "How big is your class?" and you'll get a higher average than the actual mean class size - because more students are in bigger classes!

Family Size

The average person has more siblings than average. If you survey people about family size, large families contribute more respondents.

Red Lights

Red lights seem to last longer than green. You're more likely to arrive during the longer phase of the light cycle.

Hospital Stays

A snapshot of hospital patients shows longer-than-average stays. Short stays aren't captured in point-in-time surveys.

Server Lifetimes

Current servers in a data center are older on average than the mean server lifetime - old servers accumulate while young ones are replaced.

Relationship Duration

People currently in relationships have been together longer on average. Short relationships aren't "caught" in cross-sectional samples.

The Lindy Effect

A related phenomenon: the longer something has survived, the longer it's expected to survive.

A book that's been in print for 100 years is likely to remain in print for another 100 years. A play that's been running for 10 years will likely run for 10 more. This isn't magic - it's the inspection paradox in reverse!

Things you observe NOW have already demonstrated longevity. Short-lived things have come and gone without you noticing them.
