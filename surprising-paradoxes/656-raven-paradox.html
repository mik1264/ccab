Hempel's Resolution (1940s)

Carl Hempel, who posed this paradox, actually accepted the counterintuitive conclusion! He argued that observing a green apple really does provide evidence that all ravens are black‚Äîit just seems paradoxical because we have prior knowledge (we know there are relatively few ravens and many non-black things).

Without background knowledge, in a universe where you didn't know what ravens were, checking all non-black things and finding none are ravens would legitimately confirm the hypothesis.

The Bayesian Solution

Modern probability theory offers an elegant resolution: The green apple DOES confirm "all ravens are black"‚Äîbut only by an infinitesimally small amount!

Why the evidence is tiny:

Let R = number of ravens, N = number of non-black things

Evidence from black raven ‚âà 1/R
Evidence from green apple ‚âà 1/N

Since N >> R (billions of non-black things vs. millions of ravens), the green apple's evidence is essentially negligible. Our intuition that it's "zero evidence" is wrong, but not by much!

Why This Matters

Philosophy of Science: This paradox reveals deep issues with how we define "confirmation" and "evidence." It challenges naive inductivism.

Scientific Method: Scientists often feel they're confirming theories by testing specific instances, but the logical structure of confirmation is surprisingly subtle.

The Lesson: Logic and intuition can conflict. Both "All ravens are black" and "All non-black things are non-ravens" say the same thing‚Äîbut they suggest completely different experiments!
