The Paradox of the Court

"Protagoras vs. Euathlus ‚Äî A case where both sides win AND lose"

Ancient Athens, c. 450 BCE: The great sophist Protagoras agrees to teach a poor student, Euathlus, the art of law and rhetoric‚Äîfor free, until Euathlus wins his first court case. Then he must pay the full fee.

The Problem: Euathlus completes his training but never takes a case. Years pass. Frustrated, Protagoras sues him for payment. Now watch what happens...

Protagoras demands payment for his teachings. Euathlus refuses, claiming he owes nothing. The court must decide‚Äîbut whoever rules, paradox awaits.

Both arguments are internally consistent. The paradox arises because the ruling itself affects whether the contract's condition is met!

üîÑ Why It's Paradoxical

The paradox creates a self-referential loop:

  • The contract says: "Euathlus pays when he wins his first case"
  • But the lawsuit itself could BE his first case
  • Whether the condition is met depends on the ruling
  • But the ruling should depend on whether the condition is met!

This is similar to the Liar Paradox‚Äîthe statement "This statement is false" can't be consistently evaluated because it refers to itself.

"The story goes that the Athenian jurors, utterly baffled by the reciprocal arguments (antistrephein in Greek), adjourned the case indefinitely‚Äîreportedly for 100 years!"

Whether this actually happened is debated. The paradox may be a teaching example invented by later philosophers. But the logical puzzle it presents is genuine and has been debated for over 2,400 years.

üîß Proposed Resolutions

  • Contract Terms: The contract says "first case won," which should mean a case Euathlus initiates or defends independently‚Äînot one where he's being sued for the fee itself. Protagoras's suit is premature.
  • Temporal Ordering: The court's ruling comes before it takes effect. At the moment of ruling, Euathlus hasn't yet won anything‚Äîso if the court rules for Euathlus, he doesn't owe. If for Protagoras, he does.
  • Two Trials: Some suggest Protagoras should lose this case (triggering Euathlus's first win), then immediately sue again‚Äînow the contract condition is met!
  • Reject Self-Reference: The contract shouldn't apply to a case about the contract itself, just as "this statement is false" fails because it refers to itself.

üìú Historical Context

Protagoras (c. 490‚Äì420 BCE) was the most famous of the Greek Sophists‚Äîtraveling teachers who charged fees for instruction in rhetoric, argument, and "arete" (excellence). His motto "Man is the measure of all things" encapsulated his relativism.

The paradox appears in Aulus Gellius's "Attic Nights" (2nd century CE), suggesting it circulated for centuries as a teaching example in rhetoric schools. Whether Protagoras and Euathlus were real or fictional, the logical puzzle remains a masterpiece of ancient reasoning.
