The Ancient Paradox

"The ship wherein Theseus and the youth of Athens returned from Crete had thirty oars, and was preserved by the Athenians down even to the time of Demetrius Phalereus, for they took away the old planks as they decayed, putting in new and stronger timber in their places..."
‚Äî Plutarch, Life of Theseus (1st century CE)

Plutarch reports that the Athenians preserved the ship of the legendary hero Theseus for centuries, replacing rotting planks as needed. This raised a philosophical debate: if every plank is eventually replaced, is it still the same ship that Theseus sailed?

Hobbes's Extension (1655): Thomas Hobbes made the paradox even thornier by asking: What if someone collected all the discarded original planks and rebuilt a ship from them? Now you have TWO ships‚Äîwhich one is the "real" Ship of Theseus?

Philosophical Solutions

üèõÔ∏è Aristotle's Answer

Focus on formal cause (design/function). The maintained ship keeps the same form and purpose, so it's the same ship regardless of material changes.

üîó Spatiotemporal Continuity

Identity is about continuous history. The maintained ship has an unbroken chain of existence; the rebuilt one was assembled later.

üß± Material Constitution

The rebuilt ship has the original matter. Perhaps identity is about physical substance, not history or form.

ü§∑ No Fact of the Matter

Perhaps "same ship" is just a convention. There's no objective truth‚Äîit depends on what criteria we choose to emphasize.

Why It Matters: The Ship of Theseus isn't just about boats. It challenges our intuitions about personal identity, legal ownership, restoration ethics, and what makes anything "the same" over time. The paradox has been discussed for over 2,000 years and remains unresolved!
