Newcomb's Paradox

A battle between free will and determinism that splits philosophers down the middle

The Setup

An all-knowing Predictor (call it Omega) presents you with two boxes:

  • Box A (transparent): Contains $1,000 ‚Äî you can see it
  • Box B (opaque): Contains either $1,000,000 or nothing

You have two choices:

  • One-box: Take only Box B
  • Two-box: Take both Box A and Box B

Here's the twist: The Predictor has already made its prediction and filled the boxes before you choose. If it predicted you'd one-box, Box B contains $1,000,000. If it predicted you'd two-box, Box B is empty. The Predictor has never been wrong.

What do you choose?

The Paradox

Both strategies have compelling arguments, and brilliant philosophers disagree completely:

üü£ The One-Boxer's Argument (Evidential Decision Theory)

"One-boxers leave with $1,000,000. Two-boxers leave with $1,000. The evidence is clear: one-boxing works. If the Predictor is accurate, my choice is evidence of what's in Box B. I should choose in a way that makes it likely Box B is full."

üîµ The Two-Boxer's Argument (Causal Decision Theory)

"The boxes are already filled! My choice now cannot cause money to appear or disappear. Whatever is in Box B is already there. Taking both boxes guarantees I get $1,000 more than taking only one. Two-boxing strictly dominates."

The Great Divide

When philosopher Robert Nozick introduced this problem in 1969, he found that people split roughly evenly ‚Äî and both sides thought the other was obviously wrong.

Why It Matters

Decision Theory

Newcomb's paradox sparked the development of Causal Decision Theory (CDT) and Evidential Decision Theory (EDT) as distinct frameworks. CDT says you should consider what your actions cause; EDT says you should consider what your actions are evidence of.

Free Will vs Determinism

The paradox only works if the Predictor can accurately foresee your choice. But if your choice is predictable, is it truly free? The paradox dramatizes the tension between:

  • Libertarian free will: Your choice is genuinely open until you make it
  • Determinism: Your choice is fixed by prior causes and thus predictable

AI Alignment

Modern AI researchers study Newcomb-like problems because they reveal how agents reason about other agents that model them. If an AI knows it's being predicted, how should it behave? This matters for designing trustworthy AI.

Variations and Resolutions

The Simulation Argument

Some argue that if the Predictor runs a perfect simulation of you to make its prediction, then "you" are choosing inside that simulation too. Your choice does affect Box B ‚Äî in the simulation that determines the real outcome.

Functional Decision Theory

A newer approach argues you should ask: "What decision procedure, if followed, leads to the best outcomes?" Since one-boxing leads to better outcomes for agents like you, you should one-box.

The Imperfect Predictor

If the Predictor is only 90% accurate instead of perfect, the math changes. At what accuracy does two-boxing become rational? (Answer: When accuracy drops below ~50.05%, two-boxing wins.)

What Would You Do?

The simulation above uses a perfect predictor ‚Äî your choice determines what was "already" in Box B. This may feel like cheating, but it captures the spirit of the paradox: in a deterministic universe, your "choice" and the prediction are both determined by the same prior causes.

Play the game and watch the statistics. One-boxers consistently earn more. But can you accept the seemingly irrational act of leaving guaranteed money on the table?

The deepest question: If you're the type of person who two-boxes, does that mean you're the type of person who loses? Can you decide to become a one-boxer, or is that already determined?
