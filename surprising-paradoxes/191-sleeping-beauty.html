The Debate

When Sleeping Beauty wakes up and is asked:

"What is your credence that the coin landed Heads?"

The Halfer Position (1/2)

David Lewis (2001):

Before sleep, Beauty knows P(Heads) = 1/2. When she wakes, she gains no new information about the coin. The coin was fair. Her credence should remain 1/2.

"Waking up is certain either way‚Äîit tells you nothing about the coin."

The Thirder Position (1/3)

Adam Elga (2000):

There are three possible awakening situations:

  • Heads + Monday
  • Tails + Monday
  • Tails + Tuesday

By symmetry, each is equally likely. Only one involves Heads. So P(Heads) = 1/3.

Why This Is Controversial

Both arguments seem completely valid! The puzzle touches on deep issues:

  • What counts as "new information"?
  • How should we update beliefs about our own existence?
  • Is probability about coin flips or about experiences?

The Mathematical Arguments

Thirder's Calculation

Let H = Heads, M = Monday, T = Tuesday

P(H ‚àß M) = P(T' ‚àß M) = P(T' ‚àß T)

By indifference principle, each = 1/3

Therefore: P(H) = P(H ‚àß M) = 1/3

Halfer's Calculation

P(H) = 1/2 (fair coin)

Waking up is certain in both scenarios.

P(Wake|H) = P(Wake|T') = 1

By Bayes: P(H|Wake) = P(H) = 1/2

The Dutch Book Argument

"If Beauty bets as a halfer, she can be Dutch-booked: made to accept a series of bets that guarantee a loss."
‚Äî Christopher Hitchcock

The Academic Landscape

1980s: Arnold Zuboff first formulates the problem in unpublished work.

2000: Adam Elga publishes "Self-locating belief and the Sleeping Beauty problem," arguing for 1/3.

2001: David Lewis responds with "Sleeping Beauty: reply to Elga," defending 1/2.

Today: The overwhelming majority of philosophers are thirders, but the debate continues. The problem connects to:

  • Anthropic reasoning ‚Äî How should we reason about our own existence?
  • Many-worlds interpretation ‚Äî If all outcomes occur, how do we assign probabilities?
  • Self-locating beliefs ‚Äî Uncertainty about where/when you are in the world
  • The Doomsday Argument ‚Äî Using your birth rank to estimate total humans

Why It Matters

The Sleeping Beauty problem isn't just a puzzle ‚Äî it reveals that our intuitions about probability may be fundamentally inconsistent when applied to situations involving self-location and observer selection.

The same type of reasoning applies to questions like: "What's the probability we're in a simulation?" or "What's the probability intelligent life is rare?"
