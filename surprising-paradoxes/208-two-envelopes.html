The Two Envelopes Paradox

Two envelopes contain money‚Äîone has twice as much as the other. After picking one, should you switch? Math says always switch. But then you should switch again... and again... forever!

The Setup

You're given two identical envelopes. One contains some amount of money $X, and the other contains $2X. You don't know which is which.

You pick an envelope at random. Before opening it, you're offered the chance to switch to the other envelope.

Should you switch?

Where the Logic Breaks

The flawed reasoning assumes that if your envelope contains $X, the other envelope has equal probability of containing $X/2 or $2X. But this treats "X" as two different values!

üîë The Key Insight

Let's say the envelopes contain $50 and $100. If you picked $50, the "double" is $100. If you picked $100, the "half" is $50. These are the same envelope!

The "X" in "X/2" is not the same as the "X" in "2X"‚Äîyou're comparing apples and oranges.

Correct reasoning: Call the amounts B and 2B. You have 50% chance of holding B and 50% chance of holding 2B.

E[your envelope] = 0.5 √ó B + 0.5 √ó 2B = 1.5B
E[other envelope] = 0.5 √ó 2B + 0.5 √ó B = 1.5B

Both have the same expected value! Switching gives no advantage.

Why It Feels Wrong

The paradox persists because conditioning on "what you observe" creates an asymmetry. If you see $100, you think: "The other is $50 or $200." But this requires knowing the prior distribution of amounts.

Without knowing how the amounts were chosen, you can't calculate valid probabilities. The naive 50/50 assumption is not justified‚Äîit's the source of the paradox.

"The paradox teaches us that expected value calculations require knowing the probability distribution. Assuming 50/50 for any unknown event is not always valid."

The Surprising Twist

Here's where it gets weird: there actually exist specific probability distributions where switching IS always better!

For example, if amounts are chosen as ($2k, $2k+1) with probability (2/3)k/3 for each k‚â•0, then the expected value of switching is always higher, no matter what you observe.

This doesn't violate the paradox‚Äîit shows that with the right (infinite-expectation) distribution, the "always switch" reasoning can be valid. But such distributions require infinite expected wealth, making them physically unrealizable.

Origins

The two envelopes problem emerged in the 1980s and became a classic in decision theory and Bayesian probability. It's particularly significant for the Bayesian interpretation of probability, as it highlights the importance of prior distributions.

The paradox continues to generate academic papers, with debates about the role of improper priors, infinite expectations, and the limits of rational decision-making.
