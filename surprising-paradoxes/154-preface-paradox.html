üî• THE CONTRADICTION

If you believe P‚ÇÅ AND P‚ÇÇ AND ... AND P‚Çô are all true,

üß† Why This Matters

The Preface Paradox challenges a fundamental principle of rationality called the Conjunction Principle: if you rationally believe A, and rationally believe B, then you should rationally believe "A and B."

But the author's situation shows this leads to contradiction:

  • They rationally believe each statement (99% confidence each)
  • By conjunction, they should believe ALL statements together
  • But they also rationally believe some statement is wrong (high probability)
  • These beliefs are jointly inconsistent!

üîß Proposed Solutions

1. Reject Conjunction

Henry Kyburg argued that rational belief doesn't "agglomerate" ‚Äî believing A and believing B doesn't require believing (A ‚àß B). We can tolerate "inconsistent beliefs" without believing explicit contradictions.

2. Bayesian Approach

Use degrees of belief (credences) instead of binary belief. You can assign 99% credence to each statement AND 95% credence to "there's an error" ‚Äî no contradiction, just probability theory.

3. Context-Sensitivity

When focused on statement P‚ÇÅ, you believe it. When writing the preface, you're in a different "context" evaluating the whole. Belief is fragmented across contexts.

4. Threshold Adjustment

Maybe 99% confidence isn't enough for "full belief." If you require near-certainty for belief, and account for the conjunction's lower probability, the paradox dissolves.

üìú Historical Context

The paradox was introduced by David Makinson in his 1965 paper "The Paradox of the Preface" published in Analysis.

"Suppose that in the course of writing a book a man carefully checks each assertion, convincing himself that each one is true. At the same time, he knows that he is fallible... So in the preface he writes 'any errors are my own.' This is not false modesty. He believes there are errors."
‚Äî D.C. Makinson, 1965

Makinson was inspired by Raymond Wilder's 1952 textbook Introduction to the Foundations of Mathematics, which included such a preface ‚Äî and indeed, the 1982 reprint contained three pages of errata!

The paradox is closely related to the Lottery Paradox (also proposed in the 1960s), which similarly shows how high-probability individual beliefs can combine into certain falsehood.
