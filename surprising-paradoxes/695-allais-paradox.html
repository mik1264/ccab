The Mathematical "Trick"

Choice 1: A vs B 89% shared outcome ($1M)
What differs in Choice 1: 11% certain $1M vs 10% $5M + 1% $0
Choice 2: C vs D 89% shared outcome ($0)
What differs in Choice 2: 11% $1M vs 10% $5M
The "different" parts are IDENTICAL!

üß† The Certainty Effect (Kahneman & Tversky)

Humans overweight certainty. The jump from 99% to 100% feels HUGE. The jump from 10% to 11% feels tiny. But mathematically, they're equivalent relative changes! In Choice 1, you're protecting a "sure thing." In Choice 2, you're already gambling, so why not go for the bigger prize?

üìú Historical Impact

Maurice Allais presented this paradox in 1952 to a room full of economists‚Äîincluding Paul Samuelson and other expected utility theorists. About 46% made the "irrational" choice (A and D), proving humans don't follow the independence axiom.

1944 von Neumann & Morgenstern establish Expected Utility Theory
1952 Allais presents paradox at Paris conference, breaks EU theory
1979 Kahneman & Tversky publish Prospect Theory, explain "certainty effect"
1988 Maurice Allais wins Nobel Prize in Economics
2002 Daniel Kahneman wins Nobel for behavioral economics
