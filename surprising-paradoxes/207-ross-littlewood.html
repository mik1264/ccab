The Setup: A Supertask

The vase is empty at 11:59:00 (one minute before noon). We perform infinitely many steps before noon using a clever timing scheme:

Step 1 At 30 sec before noon: Add balls 1-10, remove ball 1
Step 2 At 15 sec before noon: Add balls 11-20, remove ball 2
Step 3 At 7.5 sec before noon: Add balls 21-30, remove ball 3
Step n At 2-n min before noon: Add balls, remove ball n
Step ‚àû NOON! All steps complete. How many balls?

Resolution: The Zero Answer

The mathematical answer is zero balls. Here's why:

For the vase to contain any ball at noon, we must be able to name a specific ball number that remains. But every ball #n was removed at step n. There is no ball that escapes removal!

The intuitive "9 √ó ‚àû" argument fails because we cannot simply multiply by infinity. The cardinality of the set of balls in the vase at noon is exactly zero‚Äîit's the empty set.

The Twist: Alternative Removal Strategies

What if we change which ball we remove? Try the "Alternate" strategy above: remove ball #10, #20, #30... at each step.

Now balls 1-9, 11-19, 21-29... are never removed! At noon, the vase contains infinitely many balls. Same operations, different labeling, completely different answer!

Philosophical Implications

This paradox, formulated by John Littlewood (1953) and expanded by Sheldon Ross (1988), reveals deep questions about:

  • Supertasks: Can infinitely many tasks be completed in finite time?
  • Limits: The "limit" of a sequence may differ from intuitive extrapolation
  • Set theory: Infinity doesn't behave like large finite numbers
  • Physical reality: Can such processes exist? (Probably not!)

Philosopher Paul Benacerraf argues the problem is underspecified‚Äîwe know the state at every moment before noon, but nothing logically determines the state at noon. The vase could contain 0, ‚àû, or 42 balls‚Äîmath alone can't decide!
