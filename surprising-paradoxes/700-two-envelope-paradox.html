Two Envelope Paradox

A decision theory puzzle with no universally accepted solution

The Setup

Two envelopes contain money. One has exactly twice as much as the other.
You pick one envelope. Should you switch to the other?

Why Switching "Always" Seems Better

The Seductive Calculation

You open your envelope and see $D. The other envelope contains either:

Scenario 1 (50%)

Other has $2D

You gain +$D

Scenario 2 (50%)

Other has $D/2

You lose -$D/2

Expected value of switching:
E(switch) = 0.5 × (2D) + 0.5 × (D/2)
E(switch) = D + D/4
E(switch) = 1.25D > D ✓

Conclusion: ALWAYS switch! You gain 25% on average!

But Wait... The Infinite Loop!

If switching is always better, then after switching:

You now have the "other" envelope with some amount $D'.
By the same logic, you should switch AGAIN!
And again... and again... FOREVER!

This is clearly absurd. Where's the error?

Where the Argument Goes Wrong

Resolution #1: The "D" Represents Different Values

The fatal flaw: In the calculation, D means different things in each scenario!

If other has $2D

Your D = small amount

Total in game: $3D

If other has $D/2

Your D = large amount

Total in game: $1.5D

The game's total money changes between scenarios — but it's actually fixed! You can't legitimately add these scenarios together because they describe different games.

Resolution #2: The Fixed Total Perspective

Let T = total money in both envelopes (fixed but unknown).

Your envelope: either T/3 (small) or 2T/3 (large)
Other envelope: either 2T/3 (large) or T/3 (small)

E(switch) = 0.5 × (2T/3) + 0.5 × (T/3) = T/2
E(stay) = 0.5 × (T/3) + 0.5 × (2T/3) = T/2

E(switch) = E(stay) — No advantage to switching!

Resolution #3: Prior vs. Posterior Probability

The argument uses prior probabilities (50/50 before seeing any amount) but applies them after you know your envelope contains $D.

Once you see $D, the probabilities that you hold the small or large envelope depend on the distribution used to fill the envelopes — which is unknown!

For most reasonable distributions, seeing $D provides information that updates the 50/50 prior, breaking the symmetry of the argument.

The Infinite Mean Twist

Interestingly, there exist special probability distributions where switching IS always better — but they have infinite expected value!

Example: Envelopes contain ($2^k, $2^(k+1)) with probability (2/3)^k / 3

With this setup, switching always has higher expected value...
but E(A) = E(B) = ∞, so "∞ − ∞" is undefined!

As David Chalmers notes: "just another example of the strange behavior of infinity."

Historical Note

The Two Envelope Paradox emerged in various forms throughout the 20th century. Maurice Kraitchik published a version in 1953. The modern formulation has generated extensive academic debate, with philosophers like David Chalmers contributing analyses. Remarkably, no proposed solution is universally accepted as definitive — the debate continues in philosophy and decision theory journals to this day.
