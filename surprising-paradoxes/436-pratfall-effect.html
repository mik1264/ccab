The Pratfall Effect

Why Mistakes Make Competent People More Likeable

Perfection Is Intimidating

In 1966, social psychologist Elliot Aronson conducted an experiment that revealed something surprising about human attraction: highly competent people become more likeable after committing a blunder.

Aronson had participants listen to recordings of quiz show contestants. Some contestants scored 92% (superior) while others scored 30% (average). At the end of some recordings, the contestant could be heard spilling coffee all over themselves.

The result? The superior contestant who spilled coffee was rated most attractive of all. But the average contestant who spilled coffee was rated least attractive.

The superior person who committed a blunder was considered more attractive because the blunder humanized him, making him appear less austere and more approachable. ‚Äî Elliot Aronson, 1966

Why It Works

Humanization

Highly competent people can seem "superhuman"‚Äîintimidating and hard to relate to. A small mistake reveals that they're human after all. The distance closes, and we feel more connected to them.

Reduced Threat

Perfect people can trigger feelings of inadequacy in others. When we see them make a mistake, the threat diminishes. We no longer feel as inferior by comparison.

Authenticity

A person who makes a small blunder seems more genuine. We suspect that highly polished people are performing. The pratfall signals authenticity.

The catch: This only works for already-competent people. For average performers, mistakes confirm low expectations and make them seem even less capable. Competence is the prerequisite.

Key Insights

  • Competent people become more likeable after small mistakes
  • Average people become less likeable for the same mistakes
  • The mistake "humanizes" the superior person, reducing social distance
  • Perfection can be intimidating and alienating
