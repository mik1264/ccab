The Thought Experiment

In 1867, James Clerk Maxwell imagined a tiny intelligent being‚Äîlater called a "demon" by Lord Kelvin‚Äîguarding a trapdoor between two chambers of gas at the same temperature.

The demon watches molecules approach and opens the door only when a fast molecule comes from the left (letting it into the right chamber) or a slow molecule comes from the right (letting it into the left chamber).

After many sortings, the right chamber becomes hotter (more fast molecules) and the left becomes colder (more slow molecules)‚Äîwithout any work being done. This appears to violate the Second Law of Thermodynamics, which says entropy (disorder) in an isolated system can never decrease.

The Resolution: Information Has a Cost

Landauer's Principle (1961)
To sort molecules, the demon must measure each one‚Äîis it fast or slow? This information must be stored somewhere. But the demon's memory is finite. Eventually, it must erase old data to make room for new measurements.

Landauer's principle states that erasing one bit of information releases at least kT ln(2) joules of heat into the environment. This is thermodynamically irreversible‚Äîit increases entropy.

When you account for the entropy created by the demon's memory erasure, the total entropy of the system increases. The Second Law is saved!

Why It Matters

Maxwell's demon is more than a historical curiosity‚Äîit established that information is physical. Processing information requires energy, and erasing it produces heat. This insight underlies modern computing: every computation your computer performs generates waste heat partly because of Landauer's principle.

The demon also connects thermodynamics to quantum mechanics, biology (molecular machines that behave like demons), and even the ultimate limits of computing efficiency. Understanding the demon helps us design better nanomachines and understand how living systems manage information.
