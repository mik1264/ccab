Inattentional Blindness

The Invisible Gorilla You Never Saw

The Most Famous Psychology Demo

In 1999, psychologists Daniel Simons and Christopher Chabris created an experiment that would become one of the most famous demonstrations in psychology's history.

They showed participants a video of two teams passing basketballs and asked them to count the passes made by the team in white. Simple enough. But halfway through the video, a person in a gorilla suit walked into the scene, thumped their chest, and walked out.

The result? Approximately 50% of participants completely failed to see the gorilla‚Äî despite looking directly at it. They were so focused on counting passes that a chest-thumping gorilla became invisible.

We perceive and remember only those objects and details that receive focused attention. The gorilla was invisible because we were not looking for it. ‚Äî Simons & Chabris, 1999

Why Does This Happen?

Limited Attention Capacity

Your visual system processes enormous amounts of information, but conscious awareness is a bottleneck. You can only fully attend to a small portion of what your eyes see. Everything else is processed at a superficial level‚Äîor not at all.

Goal-Directed Attention

When you're focused on a specific task (counting passes), your attentional filter is tuned to task-relevant features (white shirts, ball movements). Anything that doesn't match your current goal can slip through unnoticed.

The Illusion of Complete Vision

The paradox: We experience a rich, detailed visual world‚Äîbut this feeling of complete awareness is itself an illusion. We see far less than we think we do. The brain fills in gaps with assumptions, creating the subjective experience of seeing "everything."

Key Insights

  • We can look directly at something and still not see it
  • Focused attention on one thing creates blindness to other things
  • Expertise doesn't protect against inattentional blindness
  • Our subjective sense of "seeing everything" is an illusion
  • Critical implications for driving, medicine, security, and more
