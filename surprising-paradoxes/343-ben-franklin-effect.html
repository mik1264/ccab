The Ben Franklin Effect

Ask for Favors to Win Friends

The Paradox

Want someone to like you? Don't do them a favor‚Äîask them for one.

This counterintuitive insight, named after Benjamin Franklin, flies in the face of common wisdom. We assume doing nice things for people makes them like us. But Franklin discovered the opposite: people who do YOU favors end up liking YOU more.

Applications

ü§ù Networking: Ask for advice, not introductions. People who give you guidance become invested in your success.

üëî Workplace: Ask a difficult colleague for help with their expertise. They'll like you more after assisting you.

üíë Dating: Request small favors on dates. Let them open the jar, recommend a restaurant, explain something. Investment breeds affection.

üìû Sales: The "foot in the door" technique starts with small asks. Each favor completed increases commitment.

The effect works in reverse: we come to hate those we've harmed.

To justify cruel actions, we dehumanize victims after the fact. Soldiers who kill may develop hatred for enemies‚Äînot before, but because of their actions. The Ben Franklin Effect explains why cycles of violence escalate: harming someone makes you dislike them more, justifying further harm.

Key Insights

The Ben Franklin Effect reveals a profound truth about human psychology: our attitudes follow our actions, not the other way around.

We think we help people we like. But really, we like people we help. The favor creates the feeling‚Äînot the reverse.
