The Easterlin Paradox

Within a country, rich people are happier. But as countries get RICHER over time, happiness stays FLAT! Money buys individual happiness but not national happiness.

üîç Key Findings

Within Countries

Rich individuals ARE happier than poor individuals. A $10K income increase boosts happiness significantly for someone earning $20K, but barely affects someone earning $200K.

Across Time

US real GDP per capita TRIPLED from 1972-2020, but average life satisfaction stayed FLAT at 7.2/10. Japan's GDP grew 5x from 1960-1990: happiness unchanged.

Across Countries

Above ~$15,000 GDP per capita, more wealth adds little happiness. Denmark (‚âà$60K) is not 4x happier than Costa Rica (‚âà$15K)‚Äîthey're about equal!

üí° Explanations

Social Comparison

We compare ourselves to neighbors, not 1950s ancestors. If everyone gets richer together, relative position stays the same‚Äîso does happiness.

Hedonic Adaptation

We adapt to new wealth levels. The excitement of a raise fades within months. The "hedonic treadmill" keeps us running in place.

Aspirations Rise

As income rises, so do expectations. Yesterday's luxury becomes today's necessity. The gap between "what I have" and "what I want" stays constant.

üí∞ The Policy Paradox

GDP growth is the central goal of economic policy worldwide. But if national happiness doesn't increase with GDP, should we optimize for something else? Bhutan measures "Gross National Happiness." New Zealand has a "Wellbeing Budget." The Easterlin Paradox challenges the foundational assumption that economic growth equals human progress.

The Discovery

Richard Easterlin published his findings in 1974 in "Does Economic Growth Improve the Human Lot?" He analyzed data from 19 countries and found no long-term relationship between GDP and happiness.

Controversy: Some economists (Stevenson & Wolfers, 2008) argue the paradox doesn't exist‚Äîthat happiness DOES rise with income, just slowly. Easterlin counters that short-term correlations don't persist long-term.

The Threshold Effect: Most researchers agree on this: money DOES buy happiness up to about $75,000/year (2020 dollars) for basic needs. Beyond that, the effect is minimal or zero.
