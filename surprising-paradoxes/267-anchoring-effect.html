üß† Why Anchoring Works

Insufficient Adjustment: We start from the anchor and adjust, but we don't adjust ENOUGH. Our brains treat the anchor as a reasonable starting point‚Äîeven when it's obviously random.

Priming: The anchor activates related memories. See "65" and you think of higher percentages; see "10" and low numbers come to mind.

Confirmation Bias: We seek evidence consistent with the anchor. High anchor? We think of reasons the answer COULD be high.

The Science

Discovered by: Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman in their 1974 paper "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." Kahneman later won the Nobel Prize in Economics (2002).

The Paradox: We KNOW the anchor is irrelevant. Participants in studies are TOLD the number is random. And yet... it still works! Even experts in their own fields fall for it.

Defense: Consider the opposite. Before estimating, ask: "What would make me think the answer is HIGHER than my anchor? What would make it LOWER?" This reduces (but doesn't eliminate) the effect.

The first number you hear isn't just information‚Äîit's an invisible hand pushing all your subsequent thoughts.
