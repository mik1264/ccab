The Mere Exposure Effect

How Familiarity Breeds Liking—Without You Knowing

Familiarity Feels Good

In 1968, Polish-American psychologist Robert Zajonc published a groundbreaking finding: simply exposing people to a stimulus—even meaningless nonsense symbols—made them like it more. No reward. No positive association. Just repetition.

Zajonc showed participants Chinese characters (to non-Chinese speakers), nonsense words, and photographs. Some appeared once, others up to 25 times. When asked to rate them afterward, participants consistently rated the frequently seen stimuli as more pleasant—even when they couldn't consciously remember seeing them before.

Preferences need no inferences. We do not have to think about stimuli to develop preferences for them; mere exposure is sufficient. — Robert Zajonc, 1980

Why Does This Happen?

The Perceptual Fluency Hypothesis

When you see something repeatedly, your brain processes it more fluently—faster and with less effort. This ease of processing is unconsciously misattributed to liking. "If it's easy to perceive," your brain reasons, "it must be good."

Evolutionary Explanation

Novel stimuli might be dangerous. Something you've seen many times without negative consequences is probably safe. This "safety signal" translates to positive affect. Familiarity = safety = liking.

The Paradox

The paradox: You believe your preferences are rational and based on objective qualities. But much of what you "like"—music, faces, brands, foods—may simply be what you've been exposed to most. Your preferences are, in part, a history of your exposures.

Real-World Applications

  • Advertising: Repetition works. Even without persuasion, brand familiarity increases purchase likelihood
  • Music: Songs become "catchy" through radio play. The more you hear it, the more you like it (up to a point)
  • Politics: Name recognition alone predicts electoral success. Simply being seen increases favorability ratings
  • Relationships: Proximity breeds attraction. People like their classmates and coworkers more than strangers—partly from mere exposure
  • Design: Familiar design patterns feel more "intuitive." Users prefer interfaces they've seen before

Limits of the Effect

The effect follows a inverted-U curve. Liking increases with exposure up to a point, then declines (tedium sets in). Negative initial reactions can be amplified by exposure. And conscious awareness of the manipulation can neutralize the effect.

Key Insights

  • Mere exposure—without any reward or positive experience—increases liking
  • The effect works even when you can't consciously remember the exposures
  • Perceptual fluency (ease of processing) is misattributed to preference
  • Much of what we "like" is simply what we've encountered most
  • Zajonc's work challenged the assumption that cognition precedes affect
