Expectations Become Reality

In a famous 1968 study at Oak School in San Francisco, researchers told teachers that certain students were "intellectual bloomers" based on a special test. These students were expected to show unusual academic gains.

At year's end, the "bloomers" had indeed improved more than their classmates.

The twist? The "bloomers" were randomly selected. There was no special test. The only difference was the teachers' expectations.

The Oak School Experiment

Robert Rosenthal and Lenore Jacobson's 1968 study "Pygmalion in the Classroom" demonstrated that teacher expectations could become self-fulfilling prophecies. Teachers treated "bloomers" differently‚Äîmore warmly, with more challenging material, more patience, and more feedback.

How Expectations Transmit

Rosenthal identified four key mechanisms:

  • Climate: Warmer emotional environment for high-expectation students
  • Input: More challenging material and complex questions
  • Output: More opportunities to respond in class
  • Feedback: More detailed, constructive feedback on performance

Beyond Education

The Pygmalion Effect appears everywhere: managers who expect more get more from employees; coaches who believe in athletes see better performance; parents' expectations shape children's achievements. The reverse‚Äîthe Golem Effect‚Äîshows that low expectations produce poor outcomes.

Criticisms and Nuance

Later research found the effect is real but modest‚Äîexpectations account for about 5-10% of student outcomes. The strongest effects appear in the first weeks of a relationship and with younger students. Still, even small expectation effects compound over years of schooling.

The Lesson

Our beliefs about others shape how we treat them, which shapes their outcomes. Expecting the best from people‚Äîgenuinely‚Äîmay be one of the most powerful things we can do for them.
