Olbers' Paradox

Why is the night sky dark?

The Deceptively Simple Question

Look up at the night sky. It's mostly dark, punctuated by stars. This seems completely obvious. But in 1823, German astronomer Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers asked a profound question:

If the universe is infinite, eternal, and filled with stars, shouldn't every line of sight eventually hit a star? Shouldn't the entire sky be as bright as the surface of a star?

The logic is simple: In an infinite universe, no matter where you look, there should be a star somewhere along that line of sight. The night sky should be blazing white, not dark.

Yet it isn't. This is Olbers' Paradox.

The Resolution

The dark night sky tells us something profound about our universe. Several factors resolve the paradox:

üåå The Universe Has a Finite Age

The Big Bang occurred about 13.8 billion years ago. Light from stars beyond ~13.8 billion light-years hasn't had time to reach us. We can only see a finite portion of the cosmos‚Äîthe "observable universe."

üî¥ The Universe is Expanding (Redshift)

Space itself is stretching. Light from distant galaxies is redshifted‚Äîstretched to longer, less energetic wavelengths. The most distant light has been shifted out of the visible spectrum entirely, into infrared and radio waves.

‚è≥ Stars Have Finite Lifetimes

Stars don't shine forever. They form, burn through their fuel, and die. The universe hasn't existed long enough for infinitely many generations of stars.

üåë Cosmic Dust Absorption

Interstellar dust absorbs starlight. However, this doesn't fully resolve the paradox‚Äîthe dust would eventually heat up and glow as brightly as the stars it absorbed.

The Profound Answer: The night sky is dark because we live in a universe with a beginning. Olbers' Paradox, formulated in 1823, was an early hint of the Big Bang‚Äîdiscovered over a century later.

The Cosmic Microwave Background

In a sense, Olbers was right! When we look far enough, we do see light from every direction. But it's not starlight‚Äîit's the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB).

The CMB is the afterglow of the Big Bang, light released 380,000 years after the universe began. It fills the entire sky uniformly. But because the universe has expanded so much, this light has been redshifted from visible light (thousands of degrees) to microwaves (just 2.7 Kelvin above absolute zero).

Original CMB temperature: ~3,000 K (glowing orange)
Current CMB temperature: 2.725 K (invisible microwaves)
The universe has expanded by a factor of ~1,100 since the CMB was released

So the sky is glowing in every direction‚Äîjust not in wavelengths our eyes can see!

A Window to Cosmology

Olbers' Paradox is remarkable because a simple observation‚Äîthe dark night sky‚Äîreveals deep truths about the cosmos:

  • The universe had a beginning (the Big Bang)
  • The universe is expanding (space itself is stretching)
  • There is a cosmic horizon (light from beyond can't reach us)
  • The universe has a temperature (the CMB at 2.7K)

Next time you look at the dark night sky, remember: that darkness is telling you the universe is finite in age, expanding, and began in a hot, dense state billions of years ago. The dark sky is one of the most profound observations in all of science.
