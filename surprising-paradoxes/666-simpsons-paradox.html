Simpson's Paradox

When aggregate trends REVERSE when data is split into groups. A statistical illusion that has fooled researchers, courts, and policymakers for decades.

TRUE VERDICT: No Discrimination (Slight Bias FOR Women!)

How Is This Possible?

The Confounding Variable: Department choice.

Women disproportionately applied to competitive humanities departments (low acceptance rates), while men disproportionately applied to science/engineering departments with more research funding (high acceptance rates).

Example: Dept A (easy, 62-82% acceptance) had 825 male applicants but only 108 female. Dept F (hard, 6-7% acceptance) had 714 female applicants but only 351 male.

Aggregating data erased this crucial context, creating the illusion of discrimination.

The Hidden Variable: Sample Size Distribution

The treatment group has 400 severe cases (where outcomes are worse for everyone), while the control group only has 100 severe cases. Even though the control wins in both subgroups, the treatment can win overall because it's "weighted down" by more severe cases!

Success(Treatment|Mild) < Success(Control|Mild) AND
Success(Treatment|Severe) < Success(Control|Severe)
BUT
Success(Treatment|Overall) > Success(Control|Overall)

Historical Context

Simpson's Paradox was first described by British statistician Edward Simpson in 1951, though mathematician Udny Yule noted similar phenomena in 1903. The Berkeley case (Bickel et al., 1975) became the canonical example in statistics education. The paradox appears in:

• Medical trials — kidney stone treatments, drug efficacy studies
• Baseball statistics — batting averages across seasons
• Economics — wage gaps, education outcomes
• COVID-19 data — vaccination effectiveness by age group

The paradox is a reminder that correlation does not imply causation, and that aggregated statistics can be deeply misleading without understanding confounding variables.
