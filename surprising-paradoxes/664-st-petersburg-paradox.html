Why Expected Value = Infinity

Each possible outcome contributes exactly $1 to the expected value:

E[X] = Σ (½)ⁿ × 2ⁿ = Σ 1 = 1 + 1 + 1 + ... = ∞
Probability × Payout = $1 for every possible outcome. Infinite outcomes → infinite sum.

The probability of needing n flips is (½)ⁿ, but the payout 2ⁿ exactly compensates. This means winning $1,000,000 (20 flips) contributes just as much to expected value as winning $2 (1 flip)!

Bernoulli's Solution: Utility Theory (1738)

Daniel Bernoulli proposed that people don't maximize expected money—they maximize expected utility. And utility has diminishing returns!

The Log-Utility Solution

If utility = log(wealth), then expected utility converges to a finite value:

E[U] ≈ log(4) ≈ 0.60 utiles

This suggests a rational person should pay at most ~$4 to play!

This insight launched the field of expected utility theory, which became foundational to economics and decision theory.

Why We Don't Pay Much

1. Diminishing Marginal Utility: Your first $1 million matters more than your second $1 million.

2. Probability Neglect: We tend to ignore extremely unlikely events (like 30+ tails in a row).

3. Bounded Wealth: No casino can actually pay $2³⁰ (~$1 billion). Real games have payout caps.

4. Risk Aversion: Humans prefer certain smaller gains to uncertain larger ones.

Median Outcome: 50% of games end on the first flip ($2). 75% end by flip 2 ($4 or less). The "infinite" expected value comes from rare astronomical wins!
