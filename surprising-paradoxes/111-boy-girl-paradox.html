ü§Ø The Tuesday Boy Twist

"A family has two children, and at least one is a boy born on a Tuesday. What's the probability both are boys?"

13/27 ‚âà 48%

Adding "born on Tuesday" changes the answer from 1/3 to nearly 1/2! This is because the additional condition further constrains the sample space. With 7 days, there are 27 (not 49) ways to have "at least one Tuesday boy" across the expanded day-of-birth combinations.

üîë The Key Insight

The common mistake is thinking: "One child is a boy, so the other has 50/50 odds."

But we're not picking a specific child‚Äîwe're filtering families. "At least one boy" includes Boy-Girl AND Girl-Boy, making Boy-Boy only 1 of 3 options.

Specifying "the older one" picks a specific child, reducing it to 2 options.

How you get the information matters as much as what the information is!

üìú History

Martin Gardner introduced this paradox in his Mathematical Games column in Scientific American in 1959. He initially gave 1/3 as the answer to Question B, but later acknowledged the ambiguity‚Äîthe answer depends on how the information was obtained.

The paradox is related to Bertrand's Box Paradox (1889) and the Monty Hall Problem, all of which reveal how human intuition fails at conditional probability.

"The way a question is phrased determines its answer."
