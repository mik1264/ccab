The Paradoxical Reasoning
❌ The Flawed Calculation
Let X = amount in your envelope

The other envelope contains either 2X or X/2 (50% each)

Expected value of switching:
E = 0.5 × (2X) + 0.5 × (X/2) = 1.25X

Conclusion: Always switch! You gain 25% on average!

But wait — if switching is always beneficial, you'd switch to B, then the same logic says switch back to A, then back to B... forever! Something is deeply wrong.

The Fatal Flaw: X Means Different Things!

In "2X" (when your envelope has less), X has a smaller expected value.
In "X/2" (when your envelope has more), X has a larger expected value.

You can't use the same X in both parts — that's like adding apples and oranges!
The "X" in each scenario refers to different probability distributions.

✓ The Correct Calculation
Let S = amount in the SMALLER envelope (fixed value)

Your envelope is equally likely to contain S or 2S

E[Your envelope] = 0.5 × S + 0.5 × 2S = 1.5S
E[Other envelope] = 0.5 × 2S + 0.5 × S = 1.5S

Conclusion: No advantage to switching! Both envelopes have the same expected value.

Historical Note

The Two Envelopes Paradox dates back to Belgian mathematician Maurice Kraitchik (1943), who posed it as a puzzle about two men comparing neckties. It reveals subtle errors in probability reasoning and the importance of proper conditioning in expected value calculations.

The paradox continues to generate philosophical debate about the nature of probability and rational decision-making.
