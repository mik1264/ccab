üß† Why Does This Work?

üéØ Asymmetric Dominance

The decoy is DOMINATED by one option (Print+Web beats Print-Only) but only PARTIALLY dominated by the other. This makes the dominating option look obviously superior.

‚öñÔ∏è Easier Comparison

Comparing Web-Only ($59) to Print+Web ($125) is hard. But comparing Print-Only to Print+Web is EASY‚Äîsame price, more stuff! The decoy provides an anchor.

‚úÖ Justification

We need to justify our choices. "I got print AND web for the same price as print alone!" feels like smart shopping, even if we never wanted print.

üåç Decoys in the Wild

üì± Phone Plans

The "medium" plan with slightly less data at nearly the same price makes the premium plan look like a bargain.

‚òï Coffee Sizes

Starbucks' "Tall" exists partly to make "Grande" seem reasonable. Few order the smallest size.

üè† Real Estate

Agents show a "fixer-upper" at a similar price before the target property‚Äîsuddenly it seems like a steal.

üíº Salary Negotiations

Presenting an extreme initial offer makes your actual target seem more reasonable.

"We don't have an internal value meter that tells us how much things are worth. Rather, we focus on the relative advantage of one thing over another, and estimate value accordingly."
‚Äî Dan Ariely, "Predictably Irrational" (2008)

üõ°Ô∏è How to Defend Against It

üîç Identify the Decoy

Ask: "Is there an option nobody would choose?" That's often the decoy steering you toward the expensive choice.

üìä Compare Pairs

Ignore the middle option and just compare the cheapest to the most expensive. What do you ACTUALLY need?

üí≠ Pre-decide

Before seeing options, decide what features you need and what you'll pay. Stick to it regardless of "deals."
