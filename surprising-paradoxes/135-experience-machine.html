You Chose to Plug In

You've chosen a life of guaranteed, maximum pleasure. In the machine, you'll experience writing the great novel, finding true love, achieving world peace‚Äîall indistinguishable from the "real" thing. You'll be happier than you could ever be outside.

This aligns with hedonism‚Äîthe view that pleasure is the only intrinsic good. If what matters is how life feels, the machine delivers perfectly.

But did you consider: Inside the machine, you won't actually do anything. You won't be a real author‚Äîyou'll just have the experience of being one. Your loved ones in the machine won't really exist. Is the experience of achievement the same as achievement itself?

You Chose to Stay Out

You've chosen reality over simulated bliss. Even knowing the machine could make you happier, you prefer an authentic life‚Äîwith all its pain, uncertainty, and struggle.

This suggests pleasure isn't the only thing you value. You care about actually doing things, not just having the experience of doing them. You care about being a certain kind of person, not just feeling like one. You care about contact with reality, even when reality hurts.

According to Nozick, this intuition‚Äîshared by roughly 84% of people in studies‚Äî refutes hedonism. If pleasure were all that mattered, we'd all plug in.

The Thought Experiment

In his 1974 book Anarchy, State, and Utopia, philosopher Robert Nozick introduced the Experience Machine as a challenge to hedonism‚Äîthe view that pleasure is the only thing that matters for a good life.

Imagine a machine that could give you any experience you desire. Super-psychologists have figured out how to stimulate your brain to make you think and feel you're writing a great novel, making a friend, reading an interesting book, or anything else you'd want. While in the tank, you won't know you're hooked up‚Äîyou'll think it's all really happening.

Would you plug in? Not just for a weekend, but for life‚Äîpreprogramming your experiences for the next two years, then choosing again, and so on?

"Suppose there were an experience machine that would give you any experience you desired. Would you plug in? What else can matter to us, other than how our lives feel from the inside?" ‚Äî Robert Nozick, Anarchy, State, and Utopia (1974)

Nozick's Three Reasons Not to Plug In

1
We Want to DO Things
We want to actually accomplish things, not just have the experience of accomplishing them. There's a difference between being a great writer and feeling like you're a great writer while floating in a tank.
2
We Want to BE Someone
We care about being a certain kind of person‚Äîcourageous, kind, creative‚Äînot just having experiences as if we were that person. Inside the machine, there's no real you being tested or developing.
3
We Want Contact with Reality
We value connection to something deeper than our own mental states. The machine limits us to a human-made reality‚Äîa closed, artificial world we've constructed for ourselves.

The Hedonist's Defense

Status Quo Bias
Perhaps we're not making a rational choice‚Äîwe're just biased toward our current state. The thought of being "plugged in with wires" feels creepy. But if we were already in the machine, would we choose to leave? Studies suggest many wouldn't.
The "Already In" Reversal
Imagine you discover you've been in the machine your whole life. Your family, career, memories‚Äîall simulated. Would you unplug into an unknown "real" world? Many people would stay, suggesting the machine isn't as obviously wrong as Nozick claims.
Imperfect Imagination
We can't truly imagine what being in the machine would be like. We picture ourselves knowing we're in a simulation‚Äîbut the whole point is you wouldn't know. Your experiences would feel completely genuine. How can we reject something we can't even imagine properly?

Why It Matters

The Experience Machine has profound implications for how we understand the good life. If most people refuse to plug in, it suggests that well-being isn't just about subjective experience‚Äîwe care about truth, authenticity, achievement, and connection to reality itself.

This connects directly to contemporary debates about virtual reality, social media, and AI. How much of our online life is "real"? If we spend hours in curated, dopamine-optimized experiences, are we approaching Nozick's machine without the wires?

The thought experiment also raises questions about what we owe each other. If you plugged in, you'd abandon the real people who need you. Is maximizing your own pleasure worth breaking real relationships?
