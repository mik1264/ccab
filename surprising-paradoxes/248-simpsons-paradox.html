THE PARADOX

Aggregate: Men admitted at higher rate (44% vs 35%)
By Department: Women admitted at higher rate in most departments!

BOTH statements are mathematically true. How is this possible?

The Confounding Variable

The paradox occurs because of a confounding variable: the choice of department. Women weren't being rejected more—they were applying to more competitive departments!

What Really Happened

Engineering Departments (A, B): High admission rates (60-80%), but few women applied.
Humanities Departments (E, F): Very low admission rates (6-28%), but many women applied.

Women were "penalized" not by discrimination, but by choosing to apply to harder departments! When you aggregate, this pattern gets hidden.

A Simple Example

The Medical Treatment Paradox

Imagine a treatment tested on two groups:

Mild cases: Treatment works 90% (9/10), Control works 80% (8/10)
Severe cases: Treatment works 30% (3/10), Control works 20% (2/10)

In BOTH groups, treatment is better. But if severe patients prefer treatment:

TreatmentControl
Mild9/10 (90%)8/10 (80%)
Severe3/90 (33%)2/10 (20%)
Total12/100 (12%)10/20 (50%)

The aggregate says control is better (50% vs 12%), but in every subgroup, treatment is better! The paradox strikes again.

When Does Simpson's Paradox Occur?

The paradox can appear whenever there's a lurking variable that affects both the predictor (gender, treatment) and the outcome (admission, recovery). It's not a bug in statistics—it's a feature that warns us: correlation is not causation!

Key Takeaways

  • Aggregated data can tell a completely different story than disaggregated data
  • Always ask: "What variable might explain both the predictor and outcome?"
  • The "correct" level of analysis depends on your causal question
  • Named by Edward Simpson (1951), but observed by Pearson (1899) and Yule (1903)
