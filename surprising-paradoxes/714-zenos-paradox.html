The Paradox (c. 450 BCE)

"In a race, the quickest runner can never overtake the slowest, since the pursuer must first reach the point whence the pursued started, so that the slower must always hold a lead."
‚Äî Aristotle, describing Zeno's argument

Zeno of Elea (c. 490‚Äì430 BCE) devised this paradox to support his teacher Parmenides's philosophy that motion and change are illusions. The argument goes:

  1. Achilles gives the tortoise a head start of 10 meters
  2. Achilles runs to where the tortoise was‚Äîbut now the tortoise has moved 1 meter ahead
  3. Achilles runs that 1 meter‚Äîbut now the tortoise has moved 0.1 meter ahead
  4. This continues forever: infinite steps = never catches up?
The Flaw: Zeno assumed that completing infinitely many tasks must take infinite time. But an infinite series can sum to a finite number! The distances 10 + 1 + 0.1 + 0.01 + ... = 11.111... meters (exactly 100/9 m).

Still Debated: Some philosophers argue that mathematics solves the calculation but not the deeper metaphysical question: How can infinitely many tasks be completed in finite time? The debate continues even today.
