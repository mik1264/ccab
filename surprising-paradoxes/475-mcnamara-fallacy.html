The McNamara Fallacy

When metrics lead you astray

"If It Can't Be Measured, It Doesn't Exist"

Robert McNamara, US Secretary of Defense from 1961-1968, believed that anything important could be quantified. He ran the Vietnam War like a business, tracking "body count ratios" as the key metric for success.

On paper, the numbers looked great. The US was winning. But in reality, they were losing the war.

This is the McNamara Fallacy: making decisions based only on what you can measure, while ignoring everything you can't ‚Äî even when the unmeasurable factors are what actually matter.

The Four Steps of the McNamara Fallacy

Daniel Yankelovich's formulation:

  1. Measure what can be easily measured. This is fine as far as it goes.
  2. Disregard what can't be easily measured or give it an arbitrary quantitative value. This is artificial and misleading.
  3. Presume that what can't be measured isn't important. This is blindness.
  4. Say that what can't be measured doesn't exist. This is suicide.
"We had all the statistics, and they were all optimistic. Every quantitative measure we had showed we were winning the war. But the statistics could not speak to will and commitment." ‚Äî James Schlesinger

The Vietnam Example: McNamara tracked body count ratios obsessively. On paper, the US was killing far more enemy soldiers than it was losing. Victory seemed assured. But what the metrics couldn't capture: the enemy's resolve, the political situation, civilian support, the narrative of the war, and the growing resistance at home.

Why This Happens: Measurable metrics are seductive because they feel objective and scientific. But many crucial factors ‚Äî morale, trust, creativity, meaning, understanding ‚Äî resist quantification. When we ignore them, we optimize for the wrong things.

Named for Robert McNamara (1916-2009), US Secretary of Defense. See: "The Fog of War" (2003), Errol Morris documentary. Related: Goodhart's Law, Campbell's Law.
