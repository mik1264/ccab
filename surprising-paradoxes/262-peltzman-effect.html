üéØ The Mechanism: Risk Homeostasis

Psychologist Gerald Wilde proposed that people have a target level of risk they're comfortable with. Safety improvements lower perceived risk, so we compensate by taking more chances‚Äîreturning to our "risk thermostat." This doesn't mean safety features are useless, but their benefits may be smaller than engineering calculations predict.

The Economics

Sam Peltzman (University of Chicago) published his provocative analysis in 1975, studying the effects of 1960s auto safety regulations. His finding‚Äîthat driver safety features increased pedestrian deaths‚Äîsparked decades of debate.

The Policy Dilemma: If safety regulations cause risk compensation, should we abandon them? Most economists say NO‚Äîthe net effect is usually still positive. But we should account for behavioral adaptation when predicting benefits.

Counterexamples: Some safety features DON'T trigger compensation‚Äîlike rear-view cameras (drivers can't really "drive more recklessly" to compensate). Passive safety that doesn't change the driving experience may be more effective.

The controversial implication: Sometimes the PERCEPTION of danger keeps us safe. Remove it at your peril.
