The Dilemma of Human Connection

In his 1851 essay collection Parerga and Paralipomena, Arthur Schopenhauer described a group of porcupines (later hedgehogs in Freud's retelling) on a cold winter's day. They huddled together for warmth‚Äîbut their quills pricked each other, forcing them apart. Yet the cold drove them back together, and the cycle repeated until they found the optimal distance: close enough for warmth, far enough to avoid pain.

Schopenhauer concluded: "The need for society which springs from the emptiness and monotony of men's lives, drives them together; but their many unpleasant and repulsive qualities and insufferable drawbacks once more drive them apart." The solution? Politeness and good manners‚Äîthe social equivalent of the hedgehogs' optimal distance.
