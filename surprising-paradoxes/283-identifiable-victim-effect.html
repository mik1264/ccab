The Paradox
People donated 2.5x more to save ONE named child than to help MILLIONS of anonymous victims. Even more disturbing: when researchers showed Rokia's story alongside the statistics, donations to Rokia dropped by 40%. Statistics don't just fail to increase giving‚Äîthey actively suppress it.

Why does this happen? Our emotional system evolved to respond to individuals, not statistics. We feel for faces, names, stories‚Äînot numbers. As numbers grow, we experience "psychic numbing": the emotional system shuts down, and we shift to cold, analytical thinking that feels nothing.

Why Numbers Numb Us

Paul Slovic calls this "psychic numbing"‚Äîour inability to feel proportionally as numbers scale up. The difference between saving 1 life and saving 2 feels meaningful. The difference between saving 100,000 and 100,001 feels like nothing.

Our emotional response follows a logarithmic curve, not a linear one. Each additional victim adds less emotional weight than the last. By the time you reach genocide-scale numbers, each life is emotionally worthless.

The dark implication: Mass atrocities may persist partly because the scale itself makes us unable to care. Charities know this‚Äîthat's why they show you ONE child with a name, not the millions they could help.

Emotion regulation matters: Research shows the collapse of compassion only happens in people who regulate their emotions. When instructed to feel freely, the effect disappears. We're not heartless‚Äîwe're protecting ourselves from overwhelming pain.
