üß† Why Does Observation Change Behavior?

üìö The Original Studies

The Illumination Experiments (1924-1927)

National Research Council studied lighting effects at Western Electric's Hawthorne Works. To their confusion, EVERY change improved productivity‚Äîeven reversing changes. The "confound" that ruined their experiment became one of psychology's most famous concepts.

The Relay Assembly Test Room (1927-1932)

Elton Mayo and Fritz Roethlisberger studied 6 women assembling telephone relays. They varied: rest breaks, work hours, free meals, piece rates. EVERY change increased productivity‚Äîeven removing the improvements! Conclusion: the attention and social dynamics, not the conditions, drove improvement.

Modern Confirmation

McCarney et al. (2007) meta-analysis found the effect across medicine, education, and workplace settings. Effect sizes vary from small to large depending on the salience of observation. Blinding in clinical trials exists specifically to control for Hawthorne effects.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Controversy: Did It Even Happen?

Modern re-analysis (Levitt & List, 2011; Olson et al., 2019) challenges the original interpretation. When they examined the original data:

  • Productivity gains were modest, not dramatic
  • Two "troublesome" workers were replaced mid-study
  • The Great Depression started during the study (economic fear ‚Üí work harder)
  • Workers may have worked harder on MONDAYS (after payday), not due to observation

The irony: The "Hawthorne Effect" may be partly a myth‚Äîyet the concept of observation changing behavior is repeatedly confirmed in other studies. The name stuck even if the original study was flawed.

üéØ Implications for Research & Management

For researchers: Use blind studies, unobtrusive measures, long acclimation periods, and control groups that are ALSO observed.

For managers: The effect eventually fades. Sustained attention works better than brief observation bursts. And sometimes the REAL improvement comes from workers feeling valued‚Äî which isn't a confound, it's a finding worth acting on.
