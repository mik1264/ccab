THIRDERS
Majority Position

Argument: There are three equally likely situations Beauty could find herself in upon waking:

  • Heads + Monday
  • Tails + Monday
  • Tails + Tuesday

From her perspective, these are indistinguishable. By symmetry, each has probability 1/3.

Therefore P(Heads) = 1/3.

‚Äî Adam Elga (2000), supported by majority of philosophers

HALFERS
Minority Position

Argument: The coin is fair, so P(Heads) = 1/2 before the experiment.

When Beauty wakes, she gains no new information about the coin flip. She knew she would be awakened regardless of the outcome.

Since no new evidence is acquired, her credence should remain P(Heads) = 1/2.

Why Is This So Controversial?

The Sleeping Beauty Problem exposes a deep ambiguity in how we think about probability and self-locating belief (beliefs about where/when you are in the world).

Thirders interpret the question as: "Given that I am experiencing an awakening, what's the probability that this awakening is a Heads-awakening?" The answer is 1/3 because Tails produces twice as many awakenings.

Halfers interpret the question as: "What's the probability the coin landed Heads?" The answer is 1/2 because the coin is fair and waking up doesn't provide new information about the flip.

The controversy persists because both interpretations seem valid, yet they give different answers. The problem touches on:

  • Self-locating belief ‚Äî How should beliefs about "when am I?" affect other beliefs?
  • Updating on indexical information ‚Äî Is "I am awake now" relevant evidence?
  • Dutch book arguments ‚Äî Which position leads to exploitable bets?
  • Anthropic reasoning ‚Äî Related to fine-tuning arguments in cosmology!

Historical Note

The problem was first posed by Arnold Zuboff in 1990 and popularized by philosopher Adam Elga in his 2000 paper "Self-locating Belief and the Sleeping Beauty Problem" in Analysis. David Lewis responded with the halfer position in 2001. Despite hundreds of papers and decades of debate, there is no consensus ‚Äî making this one of philosophy's most enduring probability puzzles. The majority of published philosophers support the thirder position, but halfers remain unconvinced.
