ü™ú The Audio Penrose Stairs

Just as the Penrose stairs seem to climb forever while returning to the same level, the Shepard Tone seems to rise forever while staying at the same average pitch.

üîÑ Octave Cycling

Each tone rises until it reaches the top, then seamlessly wraps to the bottom. Meanwhile, a new tone has already started rising below it.

üìä Volume Envelope

A bell-curve amplitude envelope peaks in the middle frequencies. High and low tones are barely audible, masking the wrap-around points.

üìú Origin: Bell Labs, 1964

Roger Shepard earned his PhD in psychology from Yale and joined Bell Labs, where he set out to create a sonic equivalent of the Penrose staircase illusion.

Using the newly invented digital sound synthesizer developed by Max Mathews, Shepard published his findings in 1964. Jean-Claude Risset later extended the technique with continuous glissando (the "Shepard-Risset glissando").

In a 1967 AT&T film, Shepard paired his tones with a visual Penrose staircase‚Äî the perfect marriage of auditory and visual impossibility.
