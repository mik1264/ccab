The Sleeping Beauty Problem

A probability puzzle that divides philosophers

The Setup: On Sunday, Sleeping Beauty is told the experiment protocol and put to sleep. A fair coin is flipped:

  • HEADS: She wakes Monday, is interviewed, and the experiment ends
  • TAILS: She wakes Monday, is interviewed, given a memory-erasing drug, sleeps, wakes Tuesday, is interviewed again

Each time she wakes, she has NO memory of previous awakenings. When interviewed, she's asked: "What is your credence that the coin landed HEADS?"

ü§î The Crucial Question

You wake up. You don't know what day it is.
What probability should you assign to HEADS?

üî¥ The Halfer Argument (David Lewis)

The coin is fair. Before the experiment, P(Heads) = 1/2. When you wake up:

  • You knew the experiment protocol beforehand
  • You gain no new information about the coin
  • Being awake doesn't change the physical coin flip
P(Heads) = P(Heads before) = 1/2
No new evidence ‚Üí No update required

Intuition: If someone asked you to bet on heads at fair odds, you should accept. The coin doesn't care how many times you wake up!

üîµ The Thirder Argument (Adam Elga)

Consider all possible "awakening events":

Heads + Monday Tails + Monday Tails + Tuesday
Three indistinguishable situations!
P = 1/3 P = 1/3 P = 1/3

By the Principle of Indifference, you should assign equal probability to each awakening. Only 1 of the 3 awakenings is a Heads-awakening.

‚à¥ P(Heads) = 1/3

üî¥ Halfer Perspective

Key Insight: Probability is about the world, not about your experiences. The coin flip happened before you woke up‚Äîyour waking state is just one sample.

Bet Test: If you bet $1 on heads at even odds, your expected value is $0 (fair). That's consistent with P(Heads) = 1/2.

üîµ Thirder Perspective

Key Insight: You should update on the fact that you're having an experience. Tails-worlds produce twice as many "you" experiences.

Frequency: Run the experiment 1000 times. ~500 heads (500 awakenings), ~500 tails (1000 awakenings). You're in a heads-awakening only 1/3 of the time!

Why This Matters

The Sleeping Beauty problem isn't just a puzzle‚Äîit touches on:

  • Self-locating beliefs: How do we reason about "which observer am I?"
  • Anthropic reasoning: Should we update on the fact that we exist?
  • Multiverse theories: If many copies of you exist, what probability do you assign to being in a particular branch?
  • Decision theory: How should we bet in scenarios with memory loss?
