Why Does This Happen?

Benford's Law emerges from the logarithmic nature of growth. Many real-world quantities grow exponentially ‚Äî populations double, investments compound, measurements span orders of magnitude.

P(first digit = d) = log‚ÇÅ‚ÇÄ(1 + 1/d)

Think about it: to go from 1 to 2, you need to grow 100%. But to go from 9 to 10, you only need to grow ~11%. Numbers "spend more time" with lower first digits because they must grow proportionally more to advance!

üîç Fraud Detection

Humans are terrible at faking Benford-distributed data! When people make up numbers, they tend to:

  • Use digits more uniformly (too many 5s, 6s, 7s)
  • Cluster below thresholds (e.g., many $9,999 expenses to avoid $10k audits)
  • Avoid repeating digits or "obvious" patterns

Real cases: Benford's Law has been used to detect fraud in Greece's EU application, Bernie Madoff's Ponzi scheme, and is admissible in US courts!

Historical Note

Astronomer Simon Newcomb first noticed in 1881 that the early pages of logarithm tables were more worn than later pages ‚Äî people looked up numbers starting with 1 and 2 more often! Physicist Frank Benford rediscovered this in 1938, analyzing 20,000+ numbers from rivers, populations, and baseball statistics. The law now bears his name and is a cornerstone of forensic accounting.
