What Happened:

  • Each person assumed the others genuinely wanted to go
  • Nobody wanted to be the one to "rock the boat"
  • They collectively did the opposite of what they all wanted
  • They blamed each other for the outcome no one wanted

The Psychology Behind the Paradox

Jerry Harvey's key insight: "Organizations frequently take actions in contradiction to what they really want to do and therefore defeat the very purposes they are trying to achieve. This is the inability to manage agreement, not disagreement."

Why Does This Happen?

Several psychological forces combine to create this paradox:

  • Fear of separation: We're terrified of being excluded from the group
  • Pluralistic ignorance: We misread others' silence as agreement
  • Action anxiety: Speaking up feels riskier than going along
  • Negative fantasies: We imagine catastrophic consequences for dissenting

"The inability to cope with agreement, not the inability to cope with conflict, is the single most pressing issue of modern organizations."

‚Äî Jerry B. Harvey, 1974

Different from Groupthink

While often confused with groupthink, the Abilene Paradox is distinct. In groupthink, people genuinely convince themselves the group decision is correct. In the Abilene Paradox, everyone privately disagrees but publicly conforms‚Äîthey never actually change their minds.
