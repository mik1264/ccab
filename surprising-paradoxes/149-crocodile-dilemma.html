The Ancient Origin

This paradox is attributed to the Stoic philosopher Chrysippus (c. 279‚Äì206 BCE) and has been discussed since ancient Greek times.

The crocodile dilemma is one of the earliest recorded examples of a self-referential paradox‚Äîa statement or situation that refers to itself in a way that creates an inescapable logical trap.

Some versions feature a father instead of a mother, or a different prediction. The key is that the parent predicts the crocodile will do the opposite of what would fulfill its promise.

Game Theory View

Modern game theory analyzes this as a scenario where the crocodile's promise creates an inconsistent "game" with no Nash equilibrium for the clever prediction.

The Deeper Lesson

The crocodile dilemma reveals problems with conditional promises that reference their own fulfillment. In everyday life, we usually avoid such constructions, but they can arise in:

  • Contracts: "I'll pay you if you prove I won't pay"
  • Predictions: Self-fulfilling or self-defeating prophecies
  • AI: Systems that must predict their own behavior
  • Law: Rules about the validity of rules

Wherever a statement or promise refers to itself, paradox lurks!
