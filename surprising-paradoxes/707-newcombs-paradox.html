One-boxers almost always get $1,000,000.

Why Is This a Paradox?

Both arguments seem airtight, yet they give opposite answers!

The Dominance Argument (Two-Boxing)

Consider the payoff table. Whatever the Predictor predicted, two-boxing gives you $1,000 more:

Two-boxing is better in every column! This is called dominance.

But wait... If the Predictor is nearly perfect, then:

  • If you're a one-boxer type ‚Üí Predictor predicted one-box ‚Üí You get $1,000,000
  • If you're a two-boxer type ‚Üí Predictor predicted two-box ‚Üí You get $1,000

One-boxers win 1000√ó more money! How can the "dominated" strategy be better?

The paradox hinges on whether your choice can be evidence about vs causally affect the prediction. If the prediction is correlated with your choice but not caused by it, which decision theory applies?

Historical Note

Physicist William Newcomb created this problem in the 1960s while at Lawrence Livermore Laboratory. Philosopher Robert Nozick analyzed it in his 1969 paper, noting he had "listened to much discussion but not satisfactorily resolved" it. Martin Gardner brought it to public attention in Scientific American (1973), reporting that among experts who responded, "weights have been about evenly balanced." The debate continues today, with causal decision theorists (two-boxers) and evidential decision theorists (one-boxers) still unable to convince each other.
