ü§î Why Is This a Paradox?

According to Expected Utility Theory, your choice between gambles should depend only on the outcomes that differ between them. The "common consequences" should be irrelevant.

The Key Insight:

Gamble 1A vs 1B has the same structure as 2A vs 2B!

1A: 89% √ó $1M + 11% √ó $1M
1B: 89% √ó $1M + 10% √ó $5M + 1% √ó $0

2A: 89% √ó $0 + 11% √ó $1M
2B: 89% √ó $0 + 10% √ó $5M + 1% √ó $0

The 89% "common consequence" changed ($1M ‚Üí $0),
but the 11% difference is IDENTICAL!

If you prefer the certainty of 1A over the risk of 1B, logically you should also prefer 2A over 2B‚Äîthe trade-off is mathematically the same! But most people don't...

Historical note: Maurice Allais presented this paradox at a 1952 Paris conference. He famously confronted Leonard Savage (co-creator of Expected Utility Theory) with these questions over lunch. Savage chose 1A and 2B‚Äîviolating his own theory! He was "deeply disturbed." Allais won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1988. This paradox helped inspire Kahneman & Tversky's Prospect Theory, for which Kahneman won the 2002 Nobel Prize.
