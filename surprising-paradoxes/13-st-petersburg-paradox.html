The St. Petersburg Paradox

A game worth infinite money that no one will pay $20 to play

The Game

In 1738, mathematician Daniel Bernoulli proposed a deceptively simple gambling game:

Flip a fair coin repeatedly until it lands on heads. Your payout doubles with each tails: $2 for heads on flip 1, $4 for heads on flip 2, $8 for flip 3, and so on. If the coin lands heads on flip n, you win $2n.

How much would you pay to play this game?

Most people say somewhere between $2 and $20. But mathematically, this game has an infinite expected value‚Äîmeaning you should be willing to pay any finite amount to play.

The Paradox

Here's the problem: nobody would pay $1,000,000 to play this game, even though the expected value far exceeds that.

In experiments, most people won't pay more than about $20. Why the massive disconnect between mathematical theory and human intuition?

Bernoulli's Solution: Diminishing Utility

Daniel Bernoulli proposed that we don't value money linearly‚Äîthe utility of money diminishes as we get more of it.

"The determination of the value of an item must not be based on the price, but rather on the utility it yields... There is no doubt that a gain of one thousand ducats is more significant to the pauper than to a rich man."
‚Äî Daniel Bernoulli, 1738

Other Proposed Solutions

1. Finite Casino Resources

No casino has infinite money. If the casino can only pay up to $1 billion, the expected value drops to about $30. If they can only pay $1 million, it's about $20.

2. Probability Neglect

Nicolas Bernoulli suggested we simply ignore events with tiny probabilities. Since the huge payoffs require astronomically unlikely sequences, we mentally round them to zero.

3. The Median vs Mean

The median outcome of the St. Petersburg game is just $2 (half the time you get heads on the first flip). Perhaps the median is a better guide than the mean for highly skewed distributions.

4. Risk Aversion

Even if expected value is infinite, the risk is also infinite. A risk-averse person rightfully demands a discount for uncertainty.

Why It Matters

The St. Petersburg paradox isn't just a mathematical curiosity‚Äîit has profound implications:

  • Insurance: Why do we buy insurance even though the expected value is negative?
  • Investing: How should we value low-probability, high-reward opportunities?
  • Climate Change: How do we weigh catastrophic but unlikely risks?
  • Existential Risk: Should we spend heavily to prevent unlikely extinction events?

The paradox reveals that expected value alone is insufficient for decision-making. We need utility theory, risk preferences, and an understanding of how probability perception works.

Historical Note

The paradox is named after the journal where Daniel Bernoulli published his solution: the Commentaries of the Imperial Academy of Science of Saint Petersburg. The problem was actually first posed by his cousin Nicolas Bernoulli in a 1713 letter to Pierre R√©mond de Montmort.

This paradox was a key catalyst in the development of expected utility theory‚Äînow fundamental to economics, finance, and decision science.
