The Logic Explained

Carl Hempel discovered this paradox in 1945. The logic is airtight:

‚úì Black Raven

Directly confirms "All ravens are black." This is intuitive‚Äîeach black raven we see makes us more confident.

‚úì Green Apple

Confirms the contrapositive "All non-black things are non-ravens." Since the statements are logically equivalent, this ALSO confirms "All ravens are black!"

‚Äî Black Shoe

Neutral. It's black but not a raven. It doesn't contradict the hypothesis, but doesn't directly confirm it either.

‚úó White Raven

DISCONFIRMS the hypothesis! A single white raven proves "All ravens are black" is false.

Why It Feels Wrong

"Should we send ornithologists to the Arctic to look for non-black ravens, or should we just sit in our armchairs cataloguing the colors of our shoes?"
‚Äî I.J. Good

The paradox feels absurd because we intuitively know that there are vastly more non-black non-ravens in the universe than there are ravens. Observing one green apple out of billions of non-black things tells us almost nothing about ravens specifically.

Proposed Solutions

Bayesian Resolution

Both observations confirm the hypothesis‚Äîbut by vastly different amounts. A black raven provides strong evidence; a green apple provides infinitesimally weak evidence. The paradox exists, but it's practically irrelevant.

Selection Effects

The paradox assumes random sampling. If you specifically sought out ravens to check their color, that's different from cataloguing random objects. The experimental design matters!

Hempel's Own View

Hempel accepted the paradox as genuine! He argued the feeling of absurdity is a "psychological illusion" caused by our background knowledge about the relative quantities of ravens vs. non-black things.

Background Knowledge

Confirmation is relative to what we already know. We know there are far fewer ravens than non-black things, so their evidential weights differ. Context matters for induction.

Why It Matters

This isn't just a logic puzzle‚Äîit reveals deep issues in the philosophy of science:

  • What counts as "evidence" for a hypothesis?
  • Can pure logic capture how science actually works?
  • How do background assumptions shape what we can learn from observations?

The raven paradox has generated hundreds of academic papers and remains a touchstone in confirmation theory, Bayesian epistemology, and the foundations of inductive reasoning.
