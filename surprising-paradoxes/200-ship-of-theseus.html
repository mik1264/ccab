The Ship of Theseus

If every plank of a ship is gradually replaced, is it still the same ship? And what if you rebuild the original from the old planks?

🤔 The Question

The Ancient Puzzle

This paradox, recorded by Plutarch in the 1st century CE, concerns the legendary ship of the Greek hero Theseus:

"The ship wherein Theseus and the youth of Athens returned from Crete had thirty oars, and was preserved by the Athenians down even to the time of Demetrius Phalerus, for they took away the old planks as they decayed, putting in new and stronger timber in their place..."
— Plutarch, "Life of Theseus"

The ship was kept in Athens for centuries as a memorial. As planks rotted, they were replaced with new ones. Eventually, every single plank had been replaced.

Plutarch then asks: "Is this still the ship of Theseus, or has it become a different ship?"

Hobbes's Twist

Philosopher Thomas Hobbes added a devastating complication in 1655:

⚒ The Second Ship

Suppose someone collected each removed plank and used them to build a second ship, arranged exactly as the original. Now we have TWO ships:

  • Ship A: The continuously maintained ship (all new planks)
  • Ship B: Rebuilt entirely from Theseus's original planks

Which one is the "real" Ship of Theseus?

Both ships have legitimate claims:

  • Ship A: Has continuity of form, function, and institutional recognition
  • Ship B: Contains all of Theseus's actual physical materials

Theories of Identity

Different philosophical positions offer different answers:

1. Spatiotemporal Continuity

Identity is preserved through continuous existence in space and time. Ship A is the real ship because it has an unbroken history. The planks are just parts, not the ship itself.

2. Material Continuity

Identity resides in the physical matter. Ship B is the real ship because it's made of the same atoms Theseus touched. New materials make a new thing.

3. Form/Pattern Theory

Identity is about structure and function, not matter. Ship A maintains the form, so it's the real ship. Both could be "Theseus's ship" if they have the same design.

4. No-Fact-of-Matter

"Same ship" is a fuzzy human concept, not a metaphysical fact. The question has no objective answer—it depends on what we care about.

Modern Variations

The Ship of Theseus appears constantly in contemporary life:

  • Your Body: Most of your cells are replaced every 7-10 years. Are you the same person you were as a child?
  • Trigger's Broom: From the British sitcom "Only Fools and Horses": a street sweeper claims he's had the same broom for 20 years—despite 17 new heads and 14 new handles.
  • Classic Cars: A restored 1965 Mustang with every part replaced—is it still a 1965 Mustang?
  • Bands: If every original member of a band is replaced, is it still the same band?
  • Companies: Is a 100-year-old company with no original employees, products, or location still the "same" company?
  • Nations: Are modern Greece and Italy the "same" as ancient Greece and Rome?

Why It Matters

The paradox reveals that our concept of "same thing" is surprisingly fragile. We use it constantly—same person, same country, same tradition—but it doesn't have clear boundaries.

💡 The Deeper Insight

"Identity" might not be a feature of the world, but a useful fiction our minds impose on continuous change. Things don't really stay "the same"—they just change slowly enough that we find it convenient to track them as if they did.

This has practical implications:

  • Legal: Copyright, inheritance, corporate liability depend on identity over time
  • Medical: Am I responsible for what "I" did before brain injury?
  • Digital: Is a copied file the "same" file? Is a restored backup the "same" system?

You Are a Ship of Theseus

Your body constantly replaces its cells:

  • Red blood cells: ~4 months
  • Skin cells: ~2-3 weeks
  • Liver cells: ~1 year
  • Skeleton: ~10 years
  • Neurons: many last a lifetime (but their atoms still cycle!)

If personal identity requires physical continuity, it's not clear you're the same person who started reading this page. Yet we feel like we persist.

This leads to classic puzzles in philosophy of mind: teleportation (are you killed and replaced?), mind uploading (does the copy have your identity?), and fission (if your brain is split, which half is "you"?).
