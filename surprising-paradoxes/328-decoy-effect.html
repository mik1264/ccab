The Decoy Effect

How an Inferior Option Makes Another Look Better

Add a strategically inferior third option, and you can shift preferences between two others.

Huber, Payne & Puto (1982) discovered this violation of rational choice theory at Duke University.

Asymmetric Dominance

The decoy is completely dominated by the target (worse in every way),
but only partially comparable to the competitor (different dimensions).

This asymmetry creates a clear "winner"‚Äîthe target‚Äîmaking the choice feel easy.

Original Research

The decoy effect was first identified at Duke University in 1982.

"An asymmetrically dominated alternative is dominated by one item in the set but not by another. Adding such an alternative to a choice set can increase the probability of choosing the item that dominates it."

‚Äî Huber, Payne & Puto, 1982
