When Skill Goes Up, Luck Matters More

Proposed by Stephen Jay Gould and popularized by Michael Mauboussin, the Paradox of Skill reveals a counterintuitive truth: as the average skill level rises, luck becomes the dominant factor in determining outcomes.

Consider: if all competitors are roughly equal in skill, then small random variations—a gust of wind, a lucky bounce, being well-rested—determine who wins. In a field of nearly identical experts, the winner is decided by chance.

This explains why:

  • No baseball player has hit .400 since Ted Williams in 1941
  • The gap between marathon gold and 20th place has shrunk dramatically
  • Most professional fund managers can't beat the market

The Last .400 Hitter

Ted Williams batted .406 in 1941. No one has hit .400 since. Are today's players worse? No—they're all much better.

In 1941, the talent pool was smaller, training was less scientific, and the skill gap between the best and average was enormous. Williams was so much better than average pitchers that he crushed them.

Today, every pitcher has access to elite training, video analysis, and specialized coaching. The gap between the best pitcher and the average has narrowed. Now even the most skilled batters face consistently elite pitching, so their batting averages cluster around a narrower range.

Absolute vs. Relative Performance

Today's average player is far more skilled than Ted Williams in absolute terms (better training, nutrition, technique). But relative to their peers, Williams had a bigger advantage. The paradox: more skill → less variation → more luck.

Where the Paradox Appears

🏆
Professional Sports
Gap between gold and 20th place has shrunk in every Olympic event. More parity means more randomness in who wins.
📈
Investing
Fewer than 15% of fund managers beat the market over 10 years. Markets are efficient because traders are all skilled.
🎲
Poker
In early poker, skilled players dominated. Now everyone reads the same books—short-term results are mostly luck.
🎓
Elite Universities
All applicants are highly qualified. Decisions often come down to essays, interviews, or timing—random factors.

What This Means

1. Focus on Process, Not Outcomes
When luck dominates, a good process can still lose and a bad process can still win. Evaluate decisions by their reasoning, not just their results.

2. Large Sample Sizes
In high-luck environments, you need many observations to identify true skill. A manager who beats the market for 3 years might just be lucky.

3. Seek Easy Games
If you must compete, find fields where skill still matters—where the skill gap is wide. Avoid fields where everyone is equally expert.

4. Accept Randomness
In mature, competitive environments, luck will play a large role. Don't overinterpret short-term outcomes or construct narratives around chance events.

The Ultimate Paradox

Success in a skill-based field leads to more skilled competitors, which makes the field more luck-based, which makes success harder to predict, which makes skill-building less rewarding... until the field collapses or new skills emerge.
