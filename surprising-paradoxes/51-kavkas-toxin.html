The Paradox Explained

ü§î Why Is This Hard?

At first it seems easy: "Just intend to drink it, get the money, then change your mind!" But can you really do that?

Gregory Kavka argued that intentions are constrained by reasons, just as beliefs are constrained by evidence. Consider:

  • You can't believe whatever you want. You can't genuinely believe the sky is green just because you'd like to.
  • Similarly, you can't intend whatever you want. You can't genuinely intend to do something when you have no reason to do it.

At the moment of drinking, there's zero reason to drink the toxin:

  • The money is already in your account (or not)‚Äîdrinking won't change that
  • Drinking will cause you pain with no compensating benefit
  • Any rational person would decline
"A rational person would know he would not drink the poison and thus could not intend to drink it." ‚Äî Gregory Kavka, 1983

Proposed Solutions

üé≠ The Self-Deception Approach

üîí Commitment Devices

Make an irrevocable commitment (e.g., bet someone you'll drink it). But the puzzle specifically asks about pure intention without external constraints.

üß† The Rationalist Solution

If intending to drink is rational (for the million), then drinking becomes rational too‚Äîthe rationality "transfers." But does it really?

üí´ Character Modification

Become the kind of person who keeps intentions regardless of consequences. But can you change your character at will?

What It Reveals About Intentions

The toxin puzzle reveals something profound about the nature of intention:

üîç Intentions Are Not Purely Voluntary

We tend to think we can intend whatever we want. But intentions are mental states that must be grounded in reasons. You can't form a genuine intention to perform an action when you know you have no reason to perform it.

This has implications for:

  • Deterrence theory: Can nations genuinely intend massive retaliation when it would be irrational to carry out?
  • Promises: Can you sincerely promise to do something you know you won't do?
  • Self-control: Why is it so hard to intend to resist temptation when you know you'll give in?
