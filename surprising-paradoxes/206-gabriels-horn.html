Imagine you have Gabriel's Horn as a real physical container. You could fill it completely with œÄ cubic units of paint‚Äîa finite amount! But if you tried to paint its inside surface with a brush, you'd need an infinite amount of paint because the surface area is infinite.

The Resolution

The paradox dissolves when we realize that paint has thickness. A real paint layer has some minimum thickness (say, a molecule). As the horn narrows, eventually it becomes thinner than a paint molecule‚Äîso physical paint couldn't coat the increasingly narrow sections.

Mathematically, surface area uses a 2D measure while volume uses 3D measure. The horn's "thickness" (in the y-direction) decreases as 1/x, fast enough to give finite volume but slow enough to give infinite surface area.

üìú Historical Note

Discovered by Evangelista Torricelli in 1641, this was one of the first examples of a solid of revolution with finite volume but infinite surface area. It shocked mathematicians of the time and contributed to the development of calculus. The name "Gabriel's Horn" comes from the biblical archangel Gabriel, who is said to blow a horn to announce Judgment Day.
