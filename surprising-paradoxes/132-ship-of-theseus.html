The Ancient Paradox

"The ship wherein Theseus and the youth of Athens returned had thirty oars, and was preserved by the Athenians... for they took away the old planks as they decayed, putting in new and stronger timber in their place."
— Plutarch, Life of Theseus (1st century CE)

Theseus was the legendary founder of Athens who slew the Minotaur. The Athenians preserved his ship for centuries as a religious relic, continually replacing rotting planks. But at what point, if any, did it stop being Theseus's actual ship?

Hobbes's Extension (1655)

Thomas Hobbes added a twist: what if someone collected all the discarded original planks and assembled them into a complete ship? Now there are TWO ships—one with the continuous form, one with the original matter. Which has the stronger claim to be "the" Ship of Theseus?

Aristotle's View: Form

The "what-it-is" of a thing is its formal cause—its design and purpose. Ship A maintains continuous identity through form, so it IS the Ship of Theseus.

Materialist View: Matter

Identity resides in physical substance. Ship B contains every atom that Theseus touched. The "maintained" ship is just a replica.

Four-Dimensionalist View

Objects are "worms" through spacetime. The original ship exists as temporal stages; Ship A and B are both continuations of that worm in different ways.

No-Identity View

Identity is a convenient fiction. Neither ship IS the original because the original no longer exists. "Same ship" is just a useful label.

You Are a Ship of Theseus

Your Body's Cells Are Constantly Replaced

Most of your body's atoms are replaced every 7-10 years. Your stomach lining regenerates every few days; red blood cells last about 4 months; even your skeleton is completely replaced every decade.

Are you the same person you were 10 years ago? You have different matter, different memories, different beliefs. What makes you "you"?

This connects to questions about teleportation (if you're disassembled and rebuilt elsewhere), brain transplants, and whether personal identity can survive radical physical change.

Modern Examples

  • Trigger's Broom (Only Fools and Horses): "Same broom for 20 years! 17 new heads, 14 new handles..."
  • George Washington's Axe: "The same axe he used to chop the cherry tree—3 new heads, 4 new handles!"
  • Classic Cars: Is a fully restored car with no original parts the "same" car?
  • Software: Is a program that's been entirely rewritten still the "same" application?
