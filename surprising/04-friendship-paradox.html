The Friendship Paradox

On average, your friends have more friends than you do. This isn't low self-esteem - it's mathematics!

A Universal Truth

In any social network (real or virtual), most people have fewer friends than their friends have on average. This isn't about you personally - it's a mathematical certainty that applies to everyone!

Why Does This Happen?

The Key Insight: Popular people appear in more people's friend lists.

When you sample "a random person's friend," you're more likely to pick someone with many friends, because they appear in many friend lists. This is sampling bias.

Think of it this way: If you have 1,000 friends, you appear in 1,000 friend lists. If I have 2 friends, I appear in only 2 friend lists. When calculating "friends of friends," you get counted 500 times more than me!

Real-World Implications

Social Media: On Facebook, Twitter, and Instagram, most people have fewer followers/friends than those they follow. This is why social media can feel isolating - you're comparing yourself to biased samples!

Disease Spread: Your friends (on average) are more socially connected, so they're likely to get infected earlier. This is used for early disease detection!

Happiness: Studies show people's friends also report being happier on average - the same sampling bias applies to many attributes correlated with social connectivity.
