The Birthday Paradox

How many people do you need in a room before there's a 50% chance two share a birthday? The answer is surprisingly small!

The Surprising Truth

Most people guess you'd need around 180+ people for a 50% chance of a shared birthday. After all, there are 365 days in a year, right?

But the actual answer is just 23 people! With only 70 people, the probability rises to 99.9%!

This counterintuitive result occurs because we're not looking for someone who shares YOUR birthday - we're looking for ANY two people who share A birthday. The number of possible pairs grows much faster than the number of people.

The Mathematics Behind It

The key insight is to calculate the probability that NO two people share a birthday, then subtract from 1.

For n people, the probability of all different birthdays is:

P(no match) = (365/365) × (364/365) × (363/365) × ... × ((365-n+1)/365)

Therefore, the probability of at least one match is:

P(match) = 1 - P(no match) = 1 - (365! / ((365-n)! × 365^n))

The number of possible pairs grows as n(n-1)/2, which is quadratic. With 23 people, there are 253 possible pairs - each one a chance for a match!
