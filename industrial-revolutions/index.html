1

First Industrial Revolution — Britain's Workshop

Britain, 1760–1840 • Steam, Coal, & Cotton

🔥 Coal & Steam Power 📚 Mechanized Textiles 🏭 Factory System 🕐 80 Years to Maturity

For 5,000 years, the world's economy ran on muscle, water, and wood. Then, in a slice of north-west England between 1760 and 1840, a confluence of cheap coal, mechanical genius (Newcomen, Watt, Arkwright, Crompton), legal certainty (the patent system), and finance broke that ceiling. The factory replaced the cottage; the steam engine replaced the horse; cotton from India replaced wool. Real wages stagnated for 50 years even as output exploded — the "Engels Pause" — before finally rising in the 1840s. The result: humanity's first sustained per-capita growth since the Neolithic.

⚙

James Watt — The Steam Engineer

1736–1819 • Glasgow Instrument Maker

An asthmatic Scotsman who repaired a Newcomen atmospheric engine model for Glasgow University in 1763 and noticed it wasted 75% of its steam in cooling. His 1769 patent for a separate condenser cut fuel consumption by two-thirds. With Birmingham industrialist Matthew Boulton, he formed Boulton & Watt in 1775 and produced ~500 engines that powered the early factories. The watt (unit of power) was named for him.

"I sell here, sir, what all the world desires to have — POWER."
— Matthew Boulton to James Boswell, 1776, on tour of the Soho Manufactory in Birmingham. Boulton & Watt engines were the iPhones of their day — and Boulton was their Steve Jobs.
"The condition of the working class is the real basis and point of departure of all social movements of the present."
— Friedrich Engels, The Condition of the Working Class in England (1845), based on his 21 months observing Manchester's mills as a young Saxon manager. Engels's father owned a Manchester cotton firm.
📚
1733 / 1764
Flying Shuttle & Spinning Jenny
John Kay's flying shuttle (1733) doubled weaving speed. James Hargreaves's spinning jenny (1764) let one operator spin 8 threads at once. Together they turned the cottage textile industry into a bottleneck waiting for industrialization.
🔥
January 5, 1769
Watt's Separate-Condenser Patent
Patent No. 913 in Edinburgh. Watt's separate condenser cuts coal consumption by ~75%. Combined with Boulton's capital and the Soho Manufactory's precision boring (1775, by John Wilkinson), the modern steam engine emerges.
🏭
1771
Cromford Mill — The First Factory
Richard Arkwright opens his water-powered cotton-spinning mill at Cromford, Derbyshire, employing 300 mostly women and children. The factory system — centralized labor, fixed hours, mechanized production — is invented here. Arkwright dies one of England's wealthiest commoners.
💸
1779
Crompton's Spinning Mule
Samuel Crompton combines the jenny and Arkwright's water frame into the spinning mule, capable of producing fine yarn at scale. He fails to patent it; sees competitors profit. Lancashire becomes the world's textile workshop.
🔥
1811–1817
The Luddite Risings
Skilled framework knitters, hand weavers, and cropmen smash machinery in Nottinghamshire, Yorkshire, and Lancashire. "Ned Ludd" — possibly mythical — gives the movement its name. The British government deploys more troops against Luddites (1812) than against Wellington in Iberia.
🚂
September 15, 1830
Liverpool & Manchester Railway Opens
The first inter-city steam railway. Stephenson's Rocket pulls passengers and freight at 30 mph. Steam moves from factory floor to landscape. Iron, coal, and capital become the foundation of a global trading empire.
📝
1844–1845
Engels in Manchester & the Communist Manifesto
Friedrich Engels publishes "The Condition of the Working Class in England" (1845), documenting Manchester's slums. With Marx, he co-writes the Communist Manifesto (Feb 1848). Industrial capitalism's contradictions enter intellectual history.
💸
Richard Arkwright (1732–1792)

Barber turned mill-owner. Patented the water frame (1769) and built the factory system at Cromford. The first true industrial capitalist millionaire.

🔥
Matthew Boulton (1728–1809)

Birmingham metalsmith and Watt's business partner. Built the Soho Manufactory, the world's most advanced engineering works of the era.

⚙
John Wilkinson (1728–1808)

"Iron-mad Jack." His 1774 cylinder-boring mill made steam-tight cylinders possible — the precision-machining breakthrough that let Watt's design actually work.

📝
Adam Smith (1723–1790)

Glasgow professor whose Wealth of Nations (1776) provided the theoretical framework for industrial capitalism: division of labor, the invisible hand, free trade.

🔥
Outcome: Britain Becomes the World's Factory (1840–1880)
By 1850 Britain produced ~50% of the world's iron and ~50% of its cotton textiles, despite holding 2% of the population. Real wages, stagnant for 50 years (the "Engels Pause"), began rising sharply from the 1840s. Life expectancy in Manchester was 26; in rural Surrey, 45. The pattern (high productivity, brutal living conditions, slow reform) became the template every subsequent industrializer would partially replicate.

⚖ Why Britain First?

Cheap coal near the surface; deep-water ports; a unified national market; a stable currency and patent system; a Protestant work ethic; the agricultural revolution freeing labor; colonial markets for cotton goods; the financial sophistication of the City. China had every ingredient except the institutional package; the Netherlands had institutions but not the coal. Britain's lottery ticket combined them all.

2

Second Industrial Revolution — The American Century Begins

Pittsburgh to Detroit, 1870–1914 • Steel, Electricity, & the Assembly Line

🔥 Bessemer Steel ⚡ Electric Power 🏭 Moving Assembly Line 💰 Carnegie, Rockefeller, Ford

Where the First Industrial Revolution mechanized cotton, the Second mechanized everything else. Henry Bessemer's converter (1856) dropped steel prices 90%. Edison's central station (1882) electrified factories and cities. Ford's moving assembly line (1913) made the car affordable. Germany and the United States overtook Britain in steel and electrical engineering by 1900. The result was the modern industrial economy: vertically-integrated trusts, scientific management, mass consumer goods, and the first true middle class.

🔥

Andrew Carnegie — The Steel King

1835–1919 • Scottish-American Industrialist

Born in Dunfermline, Scotland, emigrated penniless at 13. Worked his way up Pittsburgh railroads, then bet his fortune on Bessemer steel. Carnegie Steel produced more steel than all of Britain by 1899. Sold to J.P. Morgan for $480M in 1901 (~$15B today) and gave away ~$350M (90% of his wealth) before dying. His 1889 essay "The Gospel of Wealth" defined American industrial philanthropy.

"The man who dies thus rich, dies disgraced."
— Andrew Carnegie, "The Gospel of Wealth," North American Review, June 1889. Carnegie ultimately gave away ~$350 million — ~$11 billion today — before his death in 1919.
"Genius is one percent inspiration and ninety-nine percent perspiration."
— Thomas Edison, c. 1903, paraphrased from earlier statements. Edison's Menlo Park was the world's first industrial research lab and prototype for what would become Bell Labs and Xerox PARC.
🔥
August 24, 1856
Bessemer Patents the Converter
Henry Bessemer presents his converter at the British Association meeting in Cheltenham. By blowing air through molten iron, he removes carbon impurities in 30 minutes — a process previously taking days. Steel prices crash from $170/ton to $30/ton over two decades.
🚀
May 10, 1869
Promontory Summit — Transcontinental Railroad
Central Pacific and Union Pacific meet at Promontory, Utah. The Golden Spike is driven. The U.S. now has a unified national market 3,000 miles wide. Carnegie's steel rails replace iron rails over the next 20 years.
📱
March 10, 1876
Bell's "Mr. Watson, Come Here"
Alexander Graham Bell speaks the first telephone words to assistant Thomas Watson in the next room of his Boston lab. By 1900 there are 600,000 phones in the U.S. Bell Telephone (later AT&T) becomes the most valuable utility in history.
💡
September 4, 1882
Edison's Pearl Street Station
The world's first commercial central electric power station opens at 257 Pearl Street, Manhattan, lighting 85 customers with 400 incandescent bulbs. The DC vs. AC "war of currents" with Westinghouse and Tesla begins; AC wins by the 1890s.
🌍
February 25, 1901
U.S. Steel Created — First Billion-Dollar Corp
J.P. Morgan buys Carnegie Steel for $480M and combines it with Federal Steel and others into U.S. Steel Corporation, capitalized at $1.4 billion — the world's first billion-dollar company, controlling ~67% of U.S. steel production.
🏭
December 1, 1913
Ford's Moving Assembly Line
Highland Park, Michigan. Conveyor brings the chassis to stationary workers; assembly time drops from 12 hours to 93 minutes. Ford doubles wages to $5/day in January 1914. Mass production meets mass consumption.
🔥
1914–1918
The Great War Industrializes Total Conflict
WWI mobilizes industrial output for war on a continental scale. Haber-Bosch nitrogen fixation (developed 1909) feeds armies and feeds explosives. The German chemical industry, U.S. steel, and the dawn of aerospace all emerge from the conflict.
💰
John D. Rockefeller (1839–1937)

Standard Oil founder. Controlled ~90% of U.S. petroleum refining at peak. Broken up in 1911 antitrust ruling. Estimated net worth at death: ~$340B in 2024 dollars.

💡
Thomas Edison (1847–1931)

1,093 patents. Phonograph (1877), incandescent bulb (1879), motion-picture camera (1891). Founded GE. Ran the first industrial R&D lab at Menlo Park.

⚡
Nikola Tesla (1856–1943)

Serbian-American inventor of alternating-current electrical systems. Won the AC vs. DC "war of currents" with Westinghouse against Edison's DC.

👨‍💼
Frederick Taylor (1856–1915)

"Scientific management" pioneer. His 1911 Principles of Scientific Management codified time-and-motion studies. The factory floor would never again be the same.

🇹🇸
Outcome: The American Century Begins (1914–present)
By 1914, the U.S. produced ~40% of world manufacturing output. WWI confirmed it as the world's banker; WWII as its arsenal. The 2nd IR's package — steel, electricity, internal combustion, chemicals, mass production — would be replicated by Japan, Germany, USSR, Korea, China. By 2000, every modern factory still runs on its principles. The accompanying environmental cost (atmospheric CO₂ from ~280 ppm in 1750 to ~315 ppm in 1950 to ~425 ppm today) was the externality not yet recognized.

⚖ Comparison to the First IR

If the First IR was about replacing muscle with steam in textile production, the Second IR was about replacing skill with system. Taylor's stopwatch, Ford's assembly line, and the Carnegie/Rockefeller trusts integrated knowledge into the factory itself. America and Germany leapfrogged Britain because they industrialized later and could install the latest practices from scratch — the same advantage that East Asia would later seize.

Meiji Japan — The First Non-Western Industrializer

Japan, 1868–1912 • "Rich Country, Strong Army" (Fukoku Kyōhei)

🇯🇵 State-Led Industrialization 🌍 Iwakura Mission (1871–73) 💰 Zaibatsu Conglomerates 🕐 44 Years to Great Power

On January 3, 1868, a coalition of samurai overthrew the 265-year-old Tokugawa shogunate and "restored" the 16-year-old Emperor Meiji. They then did something no non-Western state had ever done: they deliberately, systematically, and in 40 years acquired every Western institution — constitution, navy, railways, banks, universities, factories — while keeping cultural autonomy. The Iwakura Mission (1871–73), 50 senior officials touring 12 countries for 21 months, was the most ambitious benchmarking exercise in history. By 1905 Japan defeated Russia at Tsushima.

🇯🇵

Iwakura Tomomi — Mission Leader

1825–1883 • Court Noble & Architect of Modernization

A Kōan of the imperial court who had championed the restoration. Led the 1871–1873 Iwakura Mission — 50 senior officials and students (including future Prime Ministers Itō Hirobumi and Ōkubo Toshimichi) touring the U.S., Britain, France, Germany, and 8 other nations. The mission's 100-volume report became the blueprint for Meiji modernization. Iwakura was assassinated in 1878 by samurai opposed to reform; he survived but died of cancer in 1883.

"Knowledge shall be sought throughout the world so as to strengthen the foundation of imperial rule."
— Article 5, Charter Oath of the Five Articles, April 6, 1868. Promulgated at Emperor Meiji's enthronement, this single sentence committed Japan to wholesale benchmarking from the West.
"Heaven does not create one man above or below another."
— Fukuzawa Yukichi, opening line of An Encouragement of Learning (1872), 17 volumes that sold 3.4 million copies in a country of 35 million. Fukuzawa's portrait still appears on the 10,000-yen note.
⚓
July 8, 1853
Perry's Black Ships Arrive
Commodore Matthew Perry's USS Susquehanna anchors in Edo Bay with 4 warships. Demands trade. Returns 1854 to sign the Treaty of Kanagawa, ending Japan's 220-year self-isolation. The shock will detonate the Meiji Restoration 14 years later.
🇯🇵
January 3, 1868
Meiji Restoration Proclaimed
Satsuma and Chōshū samurai overthrow the Tokugawa shogunate. The 16-year-old Emperor Meiji is "restored" to power. The Charter Oath (April 6) commits Japan to seeking knowledge worldwide.
📚
December 23, 1871–September 13, 1873
The Iwakura Mission
50 officials and 50+ students travel for 21 months across the U.S., Britain, France, Germany, and 8 other countries. They benchmark constitutions, navies, postal systems, banks, and factories. Bismarck personally lectures the delegation on realpolitik in Berlin.
💯
1872–1880
Universal Conscription & Education
January 1873: Universal male conscription, ending samurai military monopoly. 1872: National school system (4-year compulsory education for boys and girls). Both modeled on Prussian/French templates. Literacy reaches ~90% by 1900 — the highest non-Western rate.
🏭
1880s–1890s
Zaibatsu Take Over State Industries
Government sells off model factories at fire-sale prices to politically connected merchants — Mitsui, Mitsubishi, Sumitomo, Yasuda. These zaibatsu (financial cliques) become vertically integrated giants spanning shipping, banking, mining, manufacturing.
📝
February 11, 1889
Meiji Constitution Promulgated
Modeled on Prussia's 1850 constitution by Itō Hirobumi. Establishes a bicameral Diet but reserves real power for the emperor and his ministers. The legal foundation Japan would use to industrialize and to fight wars in 1894, 1904, and 1937–1945.
⚓
May 27, 1905
Battle of Tsushima — Japan Defeats Russia
Admiral Tōgō Heihachirō's Combined Fleet annihilates the Russian Baltic Fleet at the Tsushima Strait. 21 of 38 Russian ships sunk; 4,830 dead. The first time a non-Western power defeats a European great power since the rise of Western dominance. Japan's industrialization is global news.
👨‍💼
Itō Hirobumi (1841–1909)

Iwakura Mission member. First Prime Minister of Japan (1885). Wrote the Meiji Constitution. Assassinated by Korean nationalist An Jung-geun.

📚
Fukuzawa Yukichi (1835–1901)

Educator, founder of Keio University. His Encouragement of Learning was the bestseller of the era. The intellectual architect of bunmei kaika ("civilization & enlightenment").

⚙
Ōkubo Toshimichi (1830–1878)

"Bismarck of Japan." Architect of state-led industrialization. Built model cotton mills, railways, mines. Assassinated by ex-samurai in May 1878 over conscription.

💸
Iwasaki Yatarō (1835–1885)

Mitsubishi founder (1873). Started with shipping; expanded into banking, mining, shipbuilding. The model zaibatsu founder — entrepreneur tied to government contracts.

🇯🇵
Outcome: The Template for State-Led Industrialization (1905–present)
By 1905, Japan was the world's 5th naval power, the only non-white great power, and a colonial empire. The Meiji model — conservative on culture, radical on technology and institutions; state-led but with private execution; export-oriented but technology-importing — would be copied directly by South Korea (Park Chung-hee), Taiwan (Chiang Ching-kuo), and Singapore (Lee Kuan Yew). It also armed Japan for the catastrophic 1931–1945 imperial expansion.

⚖ Comparison to Britain & America

Britain industrialized bottom-up (private capital, market signals), America industrialized middle-out (entrepreneurial trusts, federal land grants), Japan industrialized top-down (Meiji oligarchs directing zaibatsu). Japan was the first nation to demonstrate that you could compress 80 years of British industrialization into 40 years if you were willing to copy ruthlessly and accept enormous social cost. China would later compress this further to 30 years; Vietnam shorter still.

Soviet Industrialization — Five-Year Plans & Magnitogorsk

USSR, 1928–1953 • Industrialization at Gunpoint

☭ Stalin's Five-Year Plans 🔥 Magnitogorsk Steel 🔥 ~6–9M Famine Deaths 🕐 25-Year Forced March

"We are 50 to 100 years behind the advanced countries," Stalin told Soviet industrial managers in February 1931. "We must close this gap in 10 years. Either we do it or we will be crushed." His forced industrialization — the first Five-Year Plan launched October 1928 — built Magnitogorsk steel city, electrified rural Russia, and made the USSR the world's second industrial power by 1939. It also collectivized agriculture by force, killing 6–9 million peasants in the resulting famines, and built a Gulag of ~18 million prisoners. The Wehrmacht in 1941 met the result of this terrible bargain.

☭

Joseph Stalin — "Man of Steel"

1878–1953 • General Secretary, CPSU

Born Iosif Dzhugashvili in Gori, Georgia. Took the alias "Stalin" (Man of Steel) in 1912. By 1928 he had outmaneuvered Trotsky, Bukharin, and Kamenev to become the USSR's unchallenged leader. His forced industrialization built one of history's largest industrial powers; his terror killed millions. He led the USSR through Operation Barbarossa, the war's eastern front, and to victory in Berlin May 1945. Died of stroke March 5, 1953, age 74.

"We are 50 or 100 years behind the advanced countries. We must make good this distance in 10 years. Either we do it, or we will be crushed."
— Joseph Stalin, speech to industrial managers, February 4, 1931. The Wehrmacht crossed the Soviet border June 22, 1941 — ten years and four months later.
"There are no fortresses that Bolsheviks cannot storm."
— Stalin, on the launch of the first Five-Year Plan, October 1928. The plan called for an 18-fold increase in heavy industry over five years; actual output rose ~150%, with the rest fictitious or counted in tonnages of poor-quality goods.
📝
October 1, 1928
First Five-Year Plan Launches
Gosplan's "optimum variant" of the plan calls for an 18% annual GDP rise. Heavy industry investment: 250%. Steel: 4M to 10M tons. The plan is announced as completed in 4.25 years — the propaganda required impossible targets.
🔥
1929–1932
Magnitogorsk Built From Scratch
100,000 workers, mostly Gulag and forced labor, build a steel city in the Urals modeled on Gary, Indiana. By 1932 it produces 1M tons of steel a year. Author John Scott, a young American Communist, documents the brutal conditions in his 1942 book Behind the Urals.
🔥
1930–1933
Forced Collectivization & the Famine
25 million peasant farms forced into ~250,000 collective farms (kolkhoz). Resistance is met with deportation; ~1.8M kulaks sent to Gulag/Siberia. The 1932–33 famine kills ~6–9M, including 3.5–5M in Ukraine (Holodomor). Grain exported to fund industrialization.
🔐
1937–1938
The Great Terror
Stalin's NKVD (Yezhov) executes ~750,000 and sends ~1.3M to the Gulag. ~30,000 Red Army officers purged, including 3 of 5 marshals. The Wehrmacht in 1941 will face an army with most of its experienced commanders murdered.
⚙
June 22, 1941
Operation Barbarossa — Test of the Industrial Base
3.8 million Wehrmacht troops invade. Soviet industry is evacuated 1,500 miles east during 1941–42 — 1,500 factories moved by rail behind the Urals. By 1942 the relocated factories are out-producing Germany in tanks, aircraft, and artillery.
🔢
1945–1953
Postwar Reconstruction & Atomic Bomb
USSR loses ~27 million in WWII; rebuilds heavy industry on slave-labor and reparations. Tests its first atomic bomb (RDS-1) August 29, 1949 — 4 years after Hiroshima. Becomes the world's second superpower by Stalin's 1953 death.
🚀
October 4, 1957
Sputnik — The Industrial Triumph's Apogee
USSR launches Sputnik 1. American Cold War panic ensues; National Defense Education Act passes 1958. Soviet industrial achievement looks ascendant — even as the rigid central planning system has already started to slow.
👨‍💼
Sergei Kirov (1886–1934)

Leningrad party boss. His December 1934 assassination — possibly orchestrated by Stalin — was the pretext for the Great Terror.

⚙
Sergo Ordzhonikidze (1886–1937)

Heavy industry commissar 1932–1937. Drove industrialization until his suicide (or murder) during the Great Terror.

📚
Aleksei Stakhanov (1906–1977)

Donbass coal miner. Mined 102 tons of coal in a single 6-hour shift, August 31, 1935 (14× the norm). The "Stakhanovite movement" of state-promoted overproduction was named for him.

🔬
Andrei Sakharov (1921–1989)

Physicist who designed the Soviet H-bomb (RDS-37, 1955), then became the USSR's most prominent dissident. 1975 Nobel Peace Prize. Internal exile in Gorky 1980–1986.

🌍
Outcome: Superpower Status at Catastrophic Cost (1945–1991)
By 1950, Soviet industrial output was ~30% of U.S. levels (up from ~5% in 1928). The USSR led the world in steel, oil, and pig-iron output by 1980. But the system was rigid, innovation-poor, and consumer-hostile. Total Stalin-era deaths: ~9–20M. The model collapsed under Gorbachev's reforms 1989–91. The lesson is brutal: industrialization can be forced from above, but at human costs that compound for generations and that ultimately produce a brittle economy.

⚖ Comparison to Britain & Japan

Britain industrialized over 80 years on bottom-up market dynamics; Japan over 40 on state-directed catch-up; the USSR over 25 by force. The Soviet model proved you could compress further than Meiji Japan if you were willing to deploy famine and Gulag — but the price was an economy that could build T-34 tanks but not consumer products and that fell apart once the terror eased. China's reform-era industrialization deliberately rejected the Stalinist model in favor of incentives and partial market discipline.

Asian Tigers — Export-Led Miracle

Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong, 1960s–1990s • The First Postwar Catch-Up

🔔 8% Annual GDP Growth 🏭 Chaebols & Conglomerates 📚 Universal Education 🕐 30 Years Per Capita 10×

Between 1960 and 1990, four small Asian economies — South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong — grew their per-capita GDP roughly tenfold. Park Chung-hee's chaebol-led developmental state in Seoul, Chiang Ching-kuo's plastics-and-electronics export drive in Taipei, Lee Kuan Yew's free-port city-state in Singapore, and Hong Kong's laissez-faire entrepot all delivered the same outcome by different means: export discipline, high savings rates, universal education, and selective protection of infant industries. The "Tigers" disproved both Marxist dependency theory and orthodox free-trade theory.

🇰🇷

Park Chung-hee — Authoritarian Architect

1917–1979 • Korean General & Dictator

Imperial Japanese Army officer who became South Korea's military strongman after the May 16, 1961 coup. Launched the first of 5 Five-Year Plans in 1962. Cultivated the chaebol — family-controlled conglomerates Hyundai, Samsung, LG, Daewoo — with cheap loans, export quotas, and protected domestic markets. Per-capita GDP rose from ~$80 (1961) to ~$1,800 (1979). Assassinated by his own intelligence chief Kim Jae-gyu, October 26, 1979.

"If you do not export, you cannot survive."
— Park Chung-hee to chaebol leaders, c. 1965. South Korea's exports rose from $55M in 1962 to $17B in 1979 — the most rapid export expansion of any economy in history at that point.
"We had to find a way to provide a reasonably good education to all Singaporeans, and the only way was to make English the working language."
— Lee Kuan Yew, in The Singapore Story (1998), on the 1965 decision to make English the language of government and business in newly-independent Singapore. The choice underwrote 50 years of foreign investment.
🇹🇼
1949–1953
Taiwan's Land Reform
KMT government on Taiwan, advised by U.S. economists, redistributes land from absentee landlords to tenant farmers. Equity-creating, productivity-boosting, and politically stabilizing. Korea undertakes similar reform 1949–50. Both cases set the foundation for export-led growth.
⚙
May 16, 1961
Park Chung-hee's Coup & First Plan
Park overthrows the elected Chang Myon government. Launches the First Five-Year Plan (Jan 1962): export-led growth, light industries first. The "Han River Miracle" begins.
🇹🇬
August 9, 1965
Singapore Becomes Independent
Expelled from Malaysia. Lee Kuan Yew weeps on TV. Inherits a port city of 1.9M, no natural resources, no hinterland. Implements: meritocracy, English-medium education, foreign investment magnet, public housing (HDB). Per-capita GDP ~$500 (1965), ~$80,000 (2024).
🏭
1973
Korea's Heavy & Chemical Industries Drive
Park's HCI plan: Korea will move from textiles to steel, ships, chemicals, electronics. POSCO (1973) becomes Asia's most efficient steel maker. Hyundai Heavy Industries opens Asia's largest shipyard 1974. Samsung Electronics 1969, expanded 1974.
💾
1980s
Taiwan's Hsinchu Park & TSMC
Hsinchu Science Park opens 1980. Morris Chang founds TSMC in 1987 with state and Philips backing — the world's first dedicated semiconductor foundry. By 2024, TSMC will produce ~90% of the world's most advanced chips.
🍼
June 29, 1987
Korea Democratizes
Mass protests force the ruling military government to accept direct presidential elections. Korea transitions to electoral democracy without giving up its export-industrial model. Taiwan democratizes through the 1990s on a similar arc.
🔥
July 2, 1997 – 1998
Asian Financial Crisis — Tigers Tested
Thai baht collapses; contagion engulfs Korea, Indonesia, Malaysia. IMF $58B Korea bailout. Daewoo collapses; chaebol consolidate. Hong Kong defends its peg. The Tigers emerge bruised but transformed — toward more transparent corporate governance.
🇹🇬
Lee Kuan Yew (1923–2015)

Singapore's founding PM (1959–1990). Built a city-state with universal English education, public housing, and zero corruption. Memoirs are required reading for every aspirant strongman.

🇹🇼
Chiang Ching-kuo (1910–1988)

Chiang Kai-shek's son. Taiwan president 1978–88. Authorized the Hsinchu Science Park, the Ten Major Construction Projects, and (decisively) the lifting of martial law in 1987.

🔥
Park Tae-joon (1927–2011)

POSCO founding chairman. Built Asia's most efficient steel mill from scratch in Pohang 1973. Korean industry's most respected technocrat.

🔘
Morris Chang (b. 1931)

TSMC founder (1987). Created the pure-play semiconductor foundry model that now powers Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, and most of the global AI hardware industry.

📉
Outcome: The Template for the China Miracle (1990–present)
By 2000, Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and Hong Kong were rich. Korea's per-capita GDP went from ~$80 (1961) to ~$36,000 (2024) — a 450× nominal increase. Their model — export discipline, technocratic state, infant-industry protection, universal education — was studied by Deng Xiaoping's reformers and applied at China's vastly larger scale from 1978. Vietnam, Bangladesh, and Indonesia have followed. The Tigers proved late development is possible.

⚖ Comparison to Earlier Industrializers

The Tigers compressed Meiji Japan's 40-year arc to ~30 years and Britain's 80 years to a single generation. They borrowed Japanese state-direction without imperialism, American consumer markets without protectionism, and Soviet planning discipline without terror. Their crucial insight: in a globalized economy, "import substitution" (Latin America's 1950s strategy) traps you behind cost-inefficient local production, while "export discipline" forces world-competitive standards on infant industries. China followed this insight at 100× the population scale.

6

Fourth Industrial Revolution — AI, IoT, & the Algorithmic Factory

Global, 2010s– • Cyber-Physical Systems & Generative AI

🤖 AI & Machine Learning 🔌 ~30B IoT Devices 🔥 Industrial 3D Printing 🕐 Davos 2016 Naming

Klaus Schwab named the Fourth Industrial Revolution at the 2016 Davos forum: a fusion of digital, physical, and biological systems — AI, robotics, IoT, 3D printing, biotech, quantum — that would reshape work and society at unprecedented speed. A decade later, the broad outlines are clear: ~30 billion connected devices, AI passing professional licensing exams (GPT-4, March 2023), industrial robots populating dark factories, and electric/autonomous vehicles displacing internal combustion. What's not yet clear: who benefits, what regulations endure, and whether the predicted productivity surge actually arrives.

🤖

Klaus Schwab — Davos Coiner

b. 1938 • Founder, World Economic Forum

German engineer and economist. Founded the European Management Forum (later World Economic Forum) in 1971. His January 2016 book "The Fourth Industrial Revolution" gave the era its now-canonical name. Critics note that 4IR is less a discrete event than the WEF's marketing term for accelerating digitalization — a continuation, not a rupture. Schwab stepped down from the WEF chair in 2024.

"The Fourth Industrial Revolution… is fundamentally different from the previous three… characterized by a fusion of technologies that is blurring the lines between the physical, digital, and biological spheres."
— Klaus Schwab, opening the 2016 World Economic Forum at Davos. The phrase "Fourth Industrial Revolution" entered the global business lexicon within 12 months.
"Software is eating the world."
— Marc Andreessen, Wall Street Journal essay, August 20, 2011. The pithier predecessor to Schwab's longer formulation. Andreessen's thesis — that every industry will become a software industry — has aged remarkably well.
📱
2007–2011
Smartphone & Cloud Foundations
iPhone (2007), Android (2008), AWS EC2 (2006), Azure (2010), and the App Store (2008) build the consumer infrastructure of the next decade. Andreessen's "software is eating the world" essay (Aug 2011) names the moment.
🤖
2012–2015
Deep Learning Comes of Age
AlexNet wins ImageNet (Sep 2012) by a 10-point margin using GPUs. Google acquires DeepMind 2014. AlphaGo defeats Lee Sedol 4-1 (March 2016). Attention Is All You Need (June 2017) introduces transformers. The AI substrate of the next decade is laid.
🌤
January 20, 2016
Davos — "Fourth Industrial Revolution" Named
Klaus Schwab's book launches at Davos. The 4IR concept becomes the official frame for 5G, IoT, AI, robotics, biotech, and additive manufacturing. National strategies follow: Germany's Industrie 4.0 (since 2011), China's Made in China 2025 (May 2015), India's Digital India.
🔌
2018–2020
5G Rollout & IoT Crosses 20 Billion
South Korea launches 5G commercially April 3, 2019. By 2020, ~20B IoT devices are connected globally. Smart cities (Singapore, Shenzhen), smart factories (Siemens Amberg "lights-out"), and connected cars become operational, not promised.
🨱
2020–2022
COVID-19 Forces Digital Transformation
Pandemic pushes ~10 years of digital adoption into 18 months. Remote work normalizes. mRNA vaccines (Moderna, Pfizer/BioNTech) demonstrate biotechnology's industrial maturity. AlphaFold 2 (DeepMind, July 2021) solves protein structure prediction.
🔥
November 30, 2022
ChatGPT & the Generative AI Boom
OpenAI's ChatGPT reaches 100M users in 2 months. GPT-4 (Mar 2023), Claude (Mar 2023), Gemini, Llama follow. Capex on AI hardware reaches $200B/year by 2024. NVIDIA's market cap goes from $300B to $3T+. The 4IR's defining technology arrives.
🛡
2024–2026
Industrial Policy Returns Globally
U.S. CHIPS Act (Aug 2022, $52B). EU Chips Act (Sep 2023). India's PLI scheme. Japan's $13B TSMC subsidy. Industrial policy — long taboo — is back as nations race for AI compute, batteries, and biotech sovereignty. The 4IR becomes geopolitical.

📊
Outcome: In Progress — The Defining Decade Is Now (2024–2035)
By 2024, AI is passing professional licensing exams, industrial robots populate ~3M factories, and EVs are 14% of new car sales. The promise: another doubling of global GDP per capita over 25 years. The risks: labor displacement, climate ceiling, regulatory capture, geopolitical fracturing into U.S./China/EU tech blocs. Unlike previous IRs, the 4IR's effects are arriving faster than institutions can adapt — making the next decade's choices about AI safety, climate, and inequality unusually consequential.

⚖ Comparison to All Five Earlier Revolutions

Where Watt's steam engine took ~70 years to deliver the modern factory, AlexNet to ChatGPT was 10 years. Where Ford's assembly line raised wages, AI's effect on labor is contested — productivity gains may flow to capital alone. Crucially, all five previous IRs raised CO₂ emissions; the 4IR is the first that may have to reduce them. The 4IR's outcome will be less determined by any one technology than by the political institutions that govern it — which is exactly the lesson of every prior wave.

Patterns Across Six Industrial Revolutions

⚙ A General-Purpose Technology Drives Each Wave

Steam (1st), electricity (2nd), the assembly line (Meiji-era adoption), heavy industry (Soviet), semiconductors (Tigers), AI (4th). Each revolution's defining tech is general-purpose — usable across sectors — not specialized. Specialized inventions never produce industrial revolutions.

🏭 The Followers Compress the Pioneer's Timeline

Britain took 80 years; America 50; Japan 40; Korea 30; China 25. Late industrializers can install the latest practices from scratch, skipping the pioneer's mistakes. The 4IR may compress further, but only for societies with the institutional and human-capital depth to absorb it.

💸 Capital Accumulates Before Wages Rise

The "Engels Pause" (1790–1840 Britain), the kulak liquidation (1930–33 USSR), and Korea's 1960s wage suppression all show real wages stagnating during initial industrialization. Wages rise only once a sustained productivity surplus is reinvested in human capital. Distribution is a political choice that lags growth.

🏭 The State Always Plays a Role

Even Britain (the supposed laissez-faire archetype) had the patent system, parliamentary enclosure, naval protection of trade, and the colonial demand sink. Pure free-market industrialization is a historical fiction. The question is not state vs. market but which state, doing what.

📚 Universal Education Is the Pre-Condition

Britain's Sunday schools; America's land-grant universities; Meiji's 1872 school act; Soviet literacy campaigns; Korea's 90% secondary enrollment by 1990. No society has industrialized successfully without first — or simultaneously — building mass education. The 4IR is no exception.

🌍 Each Wave Reorders Geopolitics

1st IR made Britain a global empire. 2nd made America a superpower. Meiji made Japan an Asian colonizer. Soviet IR enabled WWII victory. The Tigers ended European centrality in Asia. The 4IR is in the middle of remaking the U.S./China balance. Industrial capacity remains the substrate of geopolitical power.
