1

Psychoanalysis — The Unconscious Beneath

Vienna, 1890s– • Freud, the Couch, and the Talking Cure

💖 Id, Ego, Superego 💤 Dreams & Slips 💰 Berggasse 19 🕐 ~135 Years

In a Vienna apartment at Berggasse 19, a Jewish neurologist named Sigmund Freud listened to his patients on a couch and began to construct the most influential and most contested theory of the mind in modern history. Sexual drives repressed in childhood, dreams as wish-fulfillment, slips of the tongue revealing hidden meanings, the structural model of id, ego, and superego — psychoanalysis transformed psychiatry, art, and the way ordinary people talk about themselves.

💖

Sigmund Freud — Father of Psychoanalysis

1856–1939 • Viennese neurologist; founder of the Wednesday Psychological Society

Born in Freiberg, Moravia. Trained as a neurologist; studied with Charcot in Paris. With his colleague Josef Breuer published Studies on Hysteria (1895), introducing the "talking cure." Smoked twenty cigars a day; suffered jaw cancer in his last sixteen years. Fled Nazi Vienna for London in June 1938; died of physician-assisted morphine overdose in September 1939.

"The interpretation of dreams is the royal road to a knowledge of the unconscious activities of the mind."
— Sigmund Freud, The Interpretation of Dreams, 1900. Originally only 600 copies printed; took eight years to sell out.
"Where id was, there ego shall be."
— Sigmund Freud, New Introductory Lectures on Psycho-Analysis, 1933. The therapeutic project of psychoanalysis in seven words.
👪
1893–1895
The Anna O. Case
Josef Breuer treats "Anna O." (Bertha Pappenheim), discovering that talking about traumatic memories relieves hysterical symptoms. Freud and Breuer publish Studies on Hysteria in 1895; the talking cure is born.
💤
November 1899
The Interpretation of Dreams
Freud's most ambitious early work appears (dated 1900 by his publisher for prestige). Dreams are wish-fulfillments; symbols disguise repressed desires; the unconscious has its own grammar.
🏫
1902
The Wednesday Psychological Society
Freud founds a small discussion group in his Vienna apartment. Members include Adler, Stekel, and later Jung, Rank, Ferenczi, Abraham — the first generation of analysts who will spread psychoanalysis across Europe and America.
📚
September 1909
Clark University Lectures
Freud delivers five lectures at Clark University, Worcester, Massachusetts — his only U.S. visit. He brings Jung and Ferenczi. America embraces psychoanalysis as Europe never quite would.
🗣
1913–1914
The Freud-Jung Break
Carl Jung breaks with Freud over the role of sexuality and the collective unconscious. The first great schism in psychoanalysis: Jung's analytical psychology vs. Freud's orthodox line.
☮
1923
The Ego and the Id
Freud introduces his structural model: id (drives), ego (reality-testing), superego (internalized parental authority). The vocabulary of "ego" enters everyday speech.
📚
1966
Lacan's Écrits
Jacques Lacan publishes Écrits in Paris, retheorizing Freud through structural linguistics. "The unconscious is structured like a language." Psychoanalysis enters the post-structuralist era.
🧠
Carl Gustav Jung (1875–1961)

Swiss psychiatrist. Crown prince of psychoanalysis until his 1913 break with Freud. Founded analytical psychology: archetypes, collective unconscious, individuation.

🐾
Alfred Adler (1870–1937)

Vienna ophthalmologist. Broke with Freud in 1911. Individual psychology emphasized inferiority complex, social interest, and birth order.

🌹
Anna Freud (1895–1982)

Sigmund's youngest daughter. Pioneered child psychoanalysis. The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defence (1936) systematized defenses.

🗣
Jacques Lacan (1901–1981)

French analyst. Mirror stage (1936); seminars from 1953. The most influential post-Freudian outside the English-speaking world.

👫
Legacy: From Couch to Culture
Psychoanalysis as a clinical practice has shrunk dramatically since the 1970s, displaced by SSRIs and CBT. But Freud's vocabulary — ego, denial, repression, projection, Freudian slip, Oedipus complex — has become the lingua franca of self-understanding in the modern West. His influence on literature, film, art history, and feminist theory is incalculable.

⚖ Compared to Other Schools

Psychoanalysis is the antithesis of Behaviorism (which denies the unconscious), Gestalt (which denies that meaning is hidden in fragments), and Cognitive Psychology (which seeks computational, conscious processing). Yet most clinical psychotherapies today — even CBT — still use the talking, listening relationship Freud invented.

2

Behaviorism — Only Behavior Counts

America, 1913–1960s • The Stimulus-Response Revolution

🐾 Pavlov's Dogs 📚 Walden Two 🔒 Skinner Box 🕐 ~50 Years

In 1913 a young Johns Hopkins professor named John B. Watson published a manifesto: "Psychology, as the behaviorist views it, is a purely objective experimental branch of natural science." Inner mental life was to be ignored; only observable behavior — rats in mazes, pigeons pecking keys, children frightened by white rats — was scientific. Through Skinner's operant conditioning, behaviorism dominated American psychology for half a century, shaping educational technology, animal training, and modern advertising.

🔒

B. F. Skinner — The Behavioral Engineer

1904–1990 • Harvard professor; inventor of the operant chamber

Born in Susquehanna, Pennsylvania. Failed as a writer in his twenties before discovering Watson and Pavlov. At Harvard he developed the "Skinner box" for studying operant conditioning. Wrote the utopian novel Walden Two (1948) and the controversial Beyond Freedom and Dignity (1971). Refused to acknowledge inner mental states throughout his career.

"Give me a dozen healthy infants, well-formed, and my own specified world to bring them up in and I'll guarantee to take any one at random and train him to become any type of specialist I might select."
— John B. Watson, Behaviorism, 1925. The bravado that defined the school.
"A failure is not always a mistake; it may simply be the best one can do under the circumstances. The real mistake is to stop trying."
— B. F. Skinner, Beyond Freedom and Dignity, 1971. Skinner the optimistic engineer of behavior.
🐾
1897–1904
Pavlov's Conditioned Reflexes
Russian physiologist Ivan Pavlov, studying dog digestion, discovers that dogs salivate at the sound of a bell that has previously accompanied food. Classical conditioning is born; Pavlov wins the Nobel for digestion in 1904.
📝
1913
Watson's Behaviorist Manifesto
John B. Watson publishes "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It" in the Psychological Review. Introspection is unscientific; only observable behavior matters. American psychology is set on a new course.
👩💂
1920
The Little Albert Experiment
Watson and Rosalie Rayner condition an 11-month-old "Little Albert" to fear a white rat by pairing it with a loud noise. The experiment becomes infamous in psychology textbooks — and a model of what ethics committees now forbid.
🔒
1938
Skinner's Behavior of Organisms
B. F. Skinner publishes his magnum opus, distinguishing operant conditioning (behavior shaped by consequences) from Pavlovian classical conditioning. The Skinner box becomes psychology's iconic apparatus.
📚
1948
Walden Two
Skinner publishes his utopian novel set in a community engineered through positive reinforcement. Communes inspired by it (Twin Oaks, 1967) actually appear. Critics call it totalitarian; supporters call it humane.
🔦
1959
Chomsky Reviews Skinner
Noam Chomsky's devastating review of Skinner's Verbal Behavior in Language (1959) argues that operant conditioning cannot explain the productivity of human language. Behaviorism's grip on psychology begins to weaken.
📚
1971
Beyond Freedom and Dignity
Skinner's bestseller argues "freedom" and "dignity" are pre-scientific illusions; humanity's salvation lies in deliberate cultural engineering. Time magazine puts him on the cover. The cognitive revolution has already begun, but Skinner remains a public figure.
🐾
Ivan Pavlov (1849–1936)

Russian physiologist. Nobel Prize 1904 for digestion research; conditioned-reflex work made him a behaviorist hero, though he never identified as one.

👩
John B. Watson (1878–1958)

Behaviorism's founder. Forced out of Johns Hopkins in 1920 over an affair; became wildly successful at the J. Walter Thompson advertising agency.

🔬
Edward Thorndike (1874–1949)

Columbia psychologist. Puzzle-box experiments with cats (1898) yielded the Law of Effect, foreshadowing Skinner's reinforcement theory.

👩
Mary Cover Jones (1897–1987)

"Mother of behavior therapy." Her 1924 work with Peter unlearning fear of rabbits laid groundwork for systematic desensitization.

🔎
Legacy: ABA, CBT, and Slot Machines
Behaviorism declined as theory but its applications are everywhere: applied behavior analysis (ABA) for autism, cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT, the dominant evidence-based therapy), token economies in classrooms, animal training methods, gambling design (variable-ratio reinforcement schedules), and the engagement loops of social-media apps. Operant conditioning runs the contemporary attention economy.

⚖ Compared to Other Schools

Behaviorism arose explicitly against Psychoanalysis (the unobservable unconscious) and Wundtian introspection. Gestalt psychologists (Wertheimer, Köhler) rejected its atomism. The Cognitive Revolution (Miller, Chomsky, Bruner) dethroned it by reclaiming inner mental processes — though not through introspection, but through computational modeling.

3

Gestalt — The Whole Is Different From the Parts

Berlin & Frankfurt, 1910s– • Perception, Insight, and the Phi Phenomenon

👀 Phi Phenomenon 🧠 Insight Learning 🏫 Frankfurt & Berlin 🕐 ~70 Years

On a train trip to the Rhineland in summer 1910, the Czech-born psychologist Max Wertheimer noticed that two flashing lights at certain intervals appeared as one moving light. He bought a stroboscope at a Frankfurt toy shop, returned to the Psychological Institute, and began the experiments that founded Gestalt psychology. With Wolfgang Köhler and Kurt Koffka, he argued that perception, learning, and thinking were holistic processes that could not be reduced to sums of stimulus-response associations.

👀

Max Wertheimer — Founder of Gestalt

1880–1943 • Frankfurt and Berlin professor; later New School, NY

Born to a Jewish family in Prague. Studied with Hering and Stumpf. The 1912 paper "Experimental Studies on the Seeing of Motion" inaugurated Gestalt theory. Founded with Köhler and Koffka. Fled Germany in 1933 for the New School for Social Research in New York. Died of a heart attack three weeks after finishing his last book, Productive Thinking.

"The whole is something else than the sum of its parts."
— Kurt Koffka, Principles of Gestalt Psychology, 1935. Often misquoted as "greater than"; Koffka insisted on "different from."
"There are wholes, the behaviour of which is not determined by that of their individual elements, but where the part-processes are themselves determined by the intrinsic nature of the whole."
— Max Wertheimer, "Gestalt Theory," 1924. The Gestalt principle in its formal statement.
🚆
Summer 1910
The Stroboscope on the Train
Wertheimer, traveling by rail to a vacation, has the insight that two lights flashing in succession appear as one moving light if timed correctly. He gets off the train in Frankfurt to begin experiments with Köhler and Koffka as subjects.
📝
1912
The Phi Phenomenon Paper
Wertheimer publishes "Experimental Studies on the Seeing of Motion." Apparent motion (phi) cannot be reduced to component flashes; perception is a holistic gestalt. The school is officially born.
🦍
1914–1920
Köhler with the Apes of Tenerife
Marooned on Tenerife by World War I, Wolfgang Köhler studies chimpanzees at the Prussian Anthropoid Research Station. His Mentality of Apes (1917) describes "insight" learning: Sultan stacking boxes to reach a banana, suddenly grasping the solution.
📚
1922
Koffka in America
Kurt Koffka publishes "Perception: An Introduction to the Gestalt-Theorie" in Psychological Bulletin, introducing Gestalt to American psychology — then dominated by behaviorism. The reception is wary but curious.
🏫
1929–1935
The Berlin School at Its Peak
Wertheimer, Köhler, and Lewin gather brilliant students at the Berlin Psychological Institute (1922–33). Karl Duncker (problem-solving), Bluma Zeigarnik (the Zeigarnik effect), Tamara Dembo, and others extend Gestalt to motivation and cognition.
🛡
1933–1938
Flight from Nazi Germany
All three founders are Jewish or married to Jews. Wertheimer leaves for New York 1933; Koffka is already at Smith College; Köhler resists Nazi pressure for years before leaving for Swarthmore in 1935.
🌧
1947
Lewin's Field Theory
Kurt Lewin, the Berlin Gestaltist who emigrated to MIT, dies young after pioneering social psychology, action research, and group dynamics. He extends Gestalt principles from perception to social behavior.
🦍
Wolfgang Köhler (1887–1967)

Estonian-German Gestaltist. Tenerife ape research; later founded perception lab at Swarthmore. APA president 1959 in his eighties.

📚
Kurt Koffka (1886–1941)

The school's chief expositor. Principles of Gestalt Psychology (1935) is the systematic textbook.

🔥
Kurt Lewin (1890–1947)

"Father of social psychology." Topology, life space, action research, T-groups. MIT's Research Center for Group Dynamics.

📝
Bluma Zeigarnik (1901–1988)

Lithuanian-Russian Gestalt psychologist. The "Zeigarnik effect": uncompleted tasks are remembered better than completed ones.

👀
Legacy: Perception Lives, Theory Diffuses
Gestalt principles — figure/ground, proximity, similarity, closure, common fate — remain the foundation of perception research, design, UX, typography, and visual art. Insight learning anticipated cognitive psychology. Gestalt therapy (Fritz Perls, 1942–) shares only the name. The school as such dissolved in America, its members scattered, but its perceptual demonstrations are still in every textbook.

⚖ Compared to Other Schools

Gestalt opposed both Behaviorism's stimulus-response atomism and Wundtian elementalism. Where behaviorists denied insight, Köhler's apes demonstrated it. The Cognitive Revolution adopted Gestalt's holism while replacing Gestalt's vague "field forces" with computational mechanisms. Without Gestalt there is no Bruner, no Neisser.

4

Humanistic Psychology — The Third Force

California, 1950s– • Self-Actualization and Unconditional Positive Regard

🌲 Hierarchy of Needs 💙 Client-Centered 🌵 Esalen Institute 🕐 ~75 Years

In the post-war 1950s, two psychologists working independently — Abraham Maslow at Brandeis and Carl Rogers at Chicago and Wisconsin — argued that the dominant schools (psychoanalysis and behaviorism) had a stunted view of humanity. People are not driven only by neurosis or reinforcement; they have inherent tendencies toward growth, meaning, and self-actualization. Calling theirs the "Third Force," the humanistic movement gave the world client-centered therapy, the hierarchy of needs, the human potential movement, and the encounter group.

🌲

Abraham Maslow — Architect of the Pyramid

1908–1970 • Brandeis professor; founder of humanistic and transpersonal psychology

Brooklyn-born son of poor Jewish-Russian immigrants. Studied with Harry Harlow at Wisconsin (the rhesus-monkey researcher), then with Edward Thorndike at Columbia. Drafted his hierarchy of needs in a 1943 paper. Spent his last years moving from humanistic to "transpersonal" psychology, exploring peak experiences and self-transcendence. Died of a heart attack while jogging in Menlo Park.

"What a man can be, he must be. This need we may call self-actualization."
— Abraham Maslow, "A Theory of Human Motivation," Psychological Review, 1943. The pyramid first appears here.
"The curious paradox is that when I accept myself just as I am, then I can change."
— Carl Rogers, On Becoming a Person, 1961. The therapeutic principle of unconditional positive regard.
📝
1942
Rogers's Counseling and Psychotherapy
Carl Rogers publishes the first work proposing "non-directive" therapy: the client, not the therapist, leads the session. Genuineness, empathy, and unconditional positive regard heal where interpretation fails.
🌲
1943
Maslow's Hierarchy
Abraham Maslow publishes "A Theory of Human Motivation" in Psychological Review, proposing five levels of need from physiological through safety, love, esteem, to self-actualization. The pyramid graphic comes later.
📚
1954
Motivation and Personality
Maslow's first book popularizes the hierarchy and introduces the concept of "self-actualizing" personalities (Lincoln, Jefferson, Eleanor Roosevelt, Einstein, Jane Addams). Humanistic psychology has its founding text.
💭
1957–1962
The "Third Force" Named
Maslow, Rogers, May, Allport, and others convene to name themselves the "Third Force" beyond psychoanalysis and behaviorism. The Journal of Humanistic Psychology launches in 1961; the AHP forms in 1962.
📚
1961
On Becoming a Person
Rogers publishes the bestselling collection that brings client-centered therapy to a popular audience. Therapy becomes about the relationship, not the technique. CBT and humanism would later find common cause through Aaron Beck.
🌵
1962
Esalen Institute Founded
Michael Murphy and Dick Price found Esalen on the Big Sur coast. It becomes the spiritual home of the human potential movement — encounter groups, gestalt therapy (Perls), bioenergetics, and counterculture spirituality.
🌟
1969–1970
Transpersonal Psychology
Maslow, Stanislav Grof, and Anthony Sutich found the Journal of Transpersonal Psychology, extending humanistic concerns into peak experiences, mysticism, and what Maslow calls a "Fourth Force." Maslow dies a year later.
💙
Carl Rogers (1902–1987)

Wisconsin and Chicago psychologist. Client-centered therapy. Late-life work on encounter groups and conflict resolution (apartheid South Africa).

🌏
Rollo May (1909–1994)

American existential psychologist. The Meaning of Anxiety (1950); Love and Will (1969). Bridged European existentialism with American practice.

🔒
Viktor Frankl (1905–1997)

Viennese psychiatrist; Auschwitz survivor. Founded logotherapy — meaning-centered therapy. Man's Search for Meaning (1946) sold 16 million copies.

💙
James Bugental (1915–2008)

First president of AHP (1962). Existential-humanistic therapy with deeply embodied presence. Author of The Search for Authenticity (1965).

🌲
Legacy: Self-Help and Positive Psychology
Humanistic psychology lost academic prestige but transformed everyday culture: self-help books, the human potential movement, motivational speaking, the language of "growth" and "self-actualization." Martin Seligman's positive psychology (1998–) is the academic heir. Empathic, person-centered listening is now baseline best practice across all therapies. Maslow's pyramid is among the most recognized images in psychology.

⚖ Compared to Other Schools

Humanistic psychology rejected the determinism of Psychoanalysis (driven by drives) and Behaviorism (driven by reinforcement). It shared with Existentialism (May, Frankl) a focus on meaning, freedom, and authenticity, but Americanized them into hopeful self-development. The Cognitive Revolution coexisted with humanism in clinical practice; Evolutionary Psychology pushed back against humanism's "blank slate" optimism.

The Cognitive Revolution — Mind as Computer

MIT, Harvard, Stanford, 1956– • Bringing Mental Processes Back

🧠 Information Processing 📊 Magic Number 7 📝 Universal Grammar 🕐 ~70 Years

On September 11, 1956, at the MIT Symposium on Information Theory, three papers were presented: George Miller on "The Magical Number Seven," Allen Newell and Herbert Simon on the Logic Theorist, and Noam Chomsky on transformational grammar. The intellectual historian George Miller later identified that day as the birth of cognitive science. Drawing on the analogy between minds and digital computers, the cognitive revolution restored mental processes to legitimate scientific study and ended behaviorism's reign.

🧠

George A. Miller — Cognitive Revolutionary

1920–2012 • Harvard, Princeton; co-founder of cognitive science

West Virginia–born psychologist who joined Harvard's Psycho-Acoustic Lab during WWII. His 1956 paper "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two" became one of the most cited works in psychology. Co-founded Harvard's Center for Cognitive Studies in 1960 with Jerome Bruner. Created WordNet, the lexical database underlying countless NLP systems. APA president 1969.

"My problem is that I have been persecuted by an integer."
— George A. Miller, opening line of "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two," Psychological Review, 1956.
"It is fair to assume that human beings are equipped with an innate ability to acquire any human language."
— Noam Chomsky, Aspects of the Theory of Syntax, 1965. The poverty of the stimulus argument that demolished Skinner.
💻
1948–1950
Cybernetics & Information Theory
Norbert Wiener publishes Cybernetics (1948); Claude Shannon publishes the mathematical theory of communication (1948); Alan Turing's "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (1950) proposes the Turing test. The metaphor of mind as information processor is in the air.
📝
September 11, 1956
The MIT Symposium
At MIT's symposium on information theory, Miller, Newell & Simon, and Chomsky deliver the three papers that are later named the founding moment of cognitive science. None of them know it at the time.
🔦
1959
Chomsky Reviews Skinner
Noam Chomsky's review of Verbal Behavior in Language demolishes the behaviorist account of language. Children produce sentences they have never heard. The poverty of the stimulus requires innate structure.
🏫
1960
Harvard Center for Cognitive Studies
Jerome Bruner and George Miller found the Center at Harvard, which trains a generation of cognitive psychologists. The information-processing model becomes the dominant framework.
📚
1967
Neisser's Cognitive Psychology
Ulric Neisser's textbook Cognitive Psychology gives the field its name and its consolidation as a coherent enterprise. Iconic memory, perceptual cycles, top-down processing — the new vocabulary is defined.
🧠
1986
Parallel Distributed Processing
Rumelhart, McClelland, and the PDP Research Group publish the two-volume Parallel Distributed Processing, reviving connectionist neural networks. The seeds of deep learning are planted.
🔬
1990s–2010s
Cognitive Neuroscience
fMRI (1991) and other neuroimaging methods fuse cognitive psychology with neuroscience. Kahneman and Tversky's heuristics-and-biases program (Nobel 2002) brings cognitive science into economics. Behavioral economics is born.
📝
Noam Chomsky (b. 1928)

MIT linguist. Universal grammar; the language acquisition device. The most cited living scholar in the social sciences.

🏫
Jerome Bruner (1915–2016)

Harvard cognitivist; co-founder of the Center. Later turned to narrative psychology and cultural psychology. NYU emeritus until his death at 100.

🧮
Daniel Kahneman (1934–2024)

Israeli-American psychologist. With Amos Tversky founded behavioral economics. Nobel 2002. Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011).

📊
Herbert Simon (1916–2001)

Carnegie Mellon polymath. Bounded rationality; the Logic Theorist (1956), arguably the first AI program. Nobel in Economics 1978; Turing Award 1975.

🧠
Legacy: The Reigning Paradigm
Cognitive psychology is the dominant framework in academic psychology today. It generated cognitive neuroscience, cognitive behavioral therapy (Aaron Beck), behavioral economics, decision research, AI's connectionist revival (and via that, modern deep learning), human-computer interaction, and the science of memory, attention, and language. Whether the brain is "really" a computer remains contested; the metaphor's productivity is not.

⚖ Compared to Other Schools

The Cognitive Revolution explicitly overthrew Behaviorism. It absorbed Gestalt's holism but recast it computationally. Where Psychoanalysis posited dynamic unconscious drives, cognitive psychology posits implicit processing of information. CBT (Beck, Ellis) integrates cognitive theory with behaviorist techniques and humanistic warmth — the most successful synthesis in clinical psychology.

6

Evolutionary Psychology — Minds Designed by Selection

Santa Barbara & Beyond, 1990s– • The Adapted Mind

🦎 Adapted Mind 🌤 EEA 📚 Blank Slate Refuted 🕐 ~35 Years

In 1992, the anthropologist Donna Cosmides and the psychologist Leda Tooby (with Jerome Barkow) edited The Adapted Mind, declaring evolutionary psychology a coherent research program. The mind, they argued, is not a general-purpose computer but a collection of domain-specific modules shaped by natural selection in the Pleistocene "environment of evolutionary adaptedness" (EEA). Through David Buss on mating, Steven Pinker on language, and many others, evolutionary psychology became one of the most controversial and most cited movements in modern psychology.

🦎

Leda Cosmides & John Tooby — Architects of EP

b. 1957 & 1952–2023 • UC Santa Barbara; founders of the Center for Evolutionary Psychology

A psychologist (Cosmides) and an anthropologist (Tooby) who met as Harvard graduate students. Their 1989 paper "Evolutionary Psychology and the Generation of Culture" outlined the program; the 1992 book The Adapted Mind made it a movement. Cosmides's Wason selection task experiments demonstrated specialized cheater-detection reasoning. Tooby died in late 2023; Cosmides continues at UCSB.

"Our modern skulls house a Stone Age mind."
— Leda Cosmides & John Tooby, "Evolutionary Psychology: A Primer," 1997. The slogan of the school.
"The mind is not a blank slate, a noble savage, or a ghost in the machine."
— Steven Pinker, The Blank Slate, 2002. Pinker's three myths he aimed to demolish.
📚
1975
Wilson's Sociobiology
Harvard biologist E. O. Wilson publishes Sociobiology: The New Synthesis. Its final chapter applies evolutionary thinking to humans, generating fierce controversy — including a pitcher of water poured on Wilson at the AAAS in 1978.
🦊
1976
The Selfish Gene
Richard Dawkins reframes evolution from the gene's-eye view, popularizing Hamilton's inclusive fitness and Trivers's reciprocal altruism. The conceptual scaffolding for evolutionary psychology is laid.
📚
1989
Cosmides & Tooby's Manifesto
Cosmides and Tooby publish "Evolutionary Psychology and the Generation of Culture" in Ethology and Sociobiology, distinguishing EP from sociobiology and proposing massively modular minds.
📚
1992
The Adapted Mind
Barkow, Cosmides, and Tooby edit The Adapted Mind: Evolutionary Psychology and the Generation of Culture — the field's founding anthology with chapters by Daly & Wilson, Buss, Pinker, and others. EP is officially launched.
📚
1994
Buss's Evolution of Desire
David Buss publishes findings from a 37-culture study of mate preferences, demonstrating universal sex differences. EP enters popular consciousness, applauded and criticized in equal measure.
📚
1997–2002
Pinker's How the Mind Works & Blank Slate
Steven Pinker's How the Mind Works (1997) and The Blank Slate (2002) bring EP to a wide popular audience and engage public debates over human nature, sex differences, and the social sciences.
⚠
2010s
Replication Crisis Hits
EP, like much of psychology, faces failed replications and methodological critiques. Critics question reliance on WEIRD samples (Henrich 2010) and "just-so" storytelling. Defenders refine methods; the field matures.
🧡
David Buss (b. 1953)

UT Austin. Mate preferences across cultures; sexual strategies theory; jealousy. Author of Evolutionary Psychology: The New Science of the Mind.

📚
Steven Pinker (b. 1954)

Harvard psycholinguist. The Language Instinct (1994); The Blank Slate (2002). EP's most successful public communicator.

🦊
Richard Dawkins (b. 1941)

Oxford ethologist. The Selfish Gene (1976) provided the gene-centric framework EP built upon. Not strictly an EP himself.

👨
Robert Trivers (b. 1943)

Theorist of reciprocal altruism (1971), parental investment, parent-offspring conflict, and self-deception. Eccentric polymath of evolutionary theory.

🧠
Legacy: Contested but Generative
Evolutionary psychology remains controversial and politically charged but has produced robust research on mate selection, reciprocity, kin altruism, disgust, fear, language, and morality. It has reshaped behavioral ecology, anthropology, and behavioral economics. Whether human cognition is "massively modular" or more domain-general remains debated. Either way, treating the brain as a product of natural selection is now standard.

⚖ Compared to Other Schools

EP attacks the "Standard Social Science Model" implicit in Behaviorism, Humanistic, and much of Cognitive Psychology — the assumption that the mind is a general-purpose learner. Where Psychoanalysis derived universals from clinical case studies, EP derives them from cross-cultural data and adaptive logic. EP and the Cognitive Revolution share the computational framework but diverge sharply on architecture: many specialized modules vs. fewer general processes.

Key Patterns Across Schools of Psychology

🧠 The Pendulum of Inwardness

Psychology has swung between studying inner mental life (Freud, Gestalt, Cognitive, EP) and refusing to (Behaviorism). Each swing reacts against the previous school's perceived excess: behaviorism against speculative introspection, cognitive against atheoretical operationalism.

🏫 Geography of Schools

Psychoanalysis: Vienna. Behaviorism: Baltimore-Cambridge MA. Gestalt: Frankfurt-Berlin. Humanistic: Brandeis-Big Sur. Cognitive: MIT-Harvard-Stanford. Evolutionary: UC Santa Barbara. Each school is a place as much as a theory.

📚 The Cross-Disciplinary Turn

Each modern school grew by importing tools from other disciplines: psychoanalysis from neurology and literature, behaviorism from physiology, Gestalt from physics (field theory), cognitive psychology from computer science, evolutionary psychology from biology.

🌲 Therapy vs. Science Tensions

Psychoanalysis and humanistic psychology privileged clinical practice; behaviorism, cognitive psychology, and EP are research-driven. The split between clinical and academic psychology has shaped institutions, journals, and which schools "win" in any given decade.

🔬 The Replication Era

Since 2010, the replication crisis has affected all schools. Classic studies — Bandura's bobo doll, Mischel's marshmallow, Zimbardo's prison — have been re-examined. Cognitive psychology and EP have responded by tightening methods; psychoanalysis remains largely outside this discourse.

🌏 Integration and Eclecticism

Modern clinical practice draws from all schools: CBT (cognitive + behavioral), DBT (CBT + mindfulness), EMDR, ACT, schema therapy. The era of warring schools is largely over; pragmatic integration is the norm. Theoretical purity belongs to academic specialization.
