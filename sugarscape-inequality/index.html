About Sugarscape

Sugarscape is a seminal agent-based model developed by Joshua Epstein and Robert Axtell that demonstrates how economic inequality emerges from simple rules without explicit class structures or inheritance mechanisms.

Agents move across a grid harvesting sugar (resources) based on their vision (how far they can see) and metabolism (how much they consume). Despite starting with random equal endowments, profound inequality emerges through:

  • Spatial luck: Some agents start near resource-rich mountains, others in deserts
  • Vision heterogeneity: High-vision agents monopolize best patches
  • Metabolic efficiency: Low-metabolism agents accumulate wealth during good times
  • Survival selection: Better-adapted agents live longer, compounding advantages
Gini Coefficient = 0.3-0.5 (typical convergence)
0 = perfect equality | 1 = perfect inequality

Two-commodity variants add spice distributed in different peaks, enabling trade based on Cobb-Douglas welfare functions. Trade increases carrying capacity by 25-40% but also increases inequality, demonstrating the efficiency-equity tradeoff.
